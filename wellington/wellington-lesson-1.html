The Big Idea

Wellington moves on two things: wind and wheels.

Wellington's hills, narrow valleys, and windy harbour have always made getting around a puzzle. For 140 years, the city has tried steam trams, horse trams, electric trams, trolleybuses, diesel buses, and now electric double deckers and (soon) bendy buses — each one a direct response to Wellington's geography and the technology of its time. The transit map is a map of Wellington itself.

⚡ Trivia Blast

Wellington's bus fleet is lime and yellow — the same colours since the Metlink rebrand. On a busy weekday, Metlink buses carry close to 100,000 people. That's more than the entire population of Porirua!

Getting Through Wellington

Tunnels, hills, and a very famous cable car

Wellington's geography means buses are constantly negotiating tunnels. Some tunnels are over 100 years old — and one was originally built for trams.

🚇 Road Tunnels Buses Use

  • Hataitai Bus Tunnel — single-lane, 100+ years old, Pirie Street. Built for trams, now bus-only.
  • Mount Victoria Tunnel — the main SH1 route to the eastern suburbs
  • Terrace Tunnel — SH1 under the Terrace
  • Arras Tunnel — connects Basin Reserve area to the motorway
  • Karori Tunnel — too low for double deckers (hence bendy buses!)
  • Coming soon: Second Mt Victoria + Second Terrace tunnels approved 2025

🚡 The Wellington Cable Car

  • Opened 22 February 1902 — NZ's only funicular railway
  • 609 metres long, rises 120 metres up to Kelburn
  • Passes through 3 tunnels and over 3 viaducts
  • Originally steam-powered when it opened
  • 425,000 passengers in its very first year; over 1 million by 1912
  • Rebuilt as a proper funicular in 1978–79
  • Built by the Kelburne & Karori Tramway Company
⚡ Trivia Blast

The Hataitai Bus Tunnel was built over 100 years ago for trams — it's so narrow only one bus can use it at a time, and there's a traffic-light system at each end to prevent head-on collisions. Modern buses barely fit!

140 Years of Wellington Transport

From steam to electric — the long way round

Wellington's transport history is a story of constant reinvention. Each technology replaced the last as the city grew and the hills stayed just as steep.

1878 — STEAM ERA BEGINS
Wellington's first trams: steam-powered and hated
A tiny steam engine hauled a single passenger car from the Railway Station to Adelaide Road, with a branch to Queens Wharf. They were a disaster — belching smoke and fumes, terrifying horses pulling cabs, and hammering the rails so hard they needed constant repair. Passengers complained about the noise and the smell.
1882 — HORSE POWER
Horse-drawn trams replace steam
The heavy steam trams gave way to horse-drawn trams, which Wellingtonians actually liked. Routes extended to Tinakori Road and Courtenay Place. Much quieter, much less smelly (well, mostly), and the horses didn't terrify other horses.
1900 — CITY COUNCIL TAKES OVER
Wellington City Corporation buys the tramways
The Wellington City Corporation (WCC) purchased the Wellington City Tramways Company. Two years later, the council decided to electrify — because Wellington had plenty of hydroelectric power from the Wairarapa rivers.
1902 — CABLE CAR OPENS
The cable car climbs to Kelburn for the first time
On 22 February 1902, the Kelburne & Karori Tramway Company opened the cable car — originally steam-powered, later electric. It was an immediate hit: 425,000 riders in year one, over a million by 1912. It opened up Kelburn for residential development and is still running today, 120+ years later.
1904 — ELECTRIC TRAMS ARRIVE
30 June 1904: the first electric tram runs
The first electric tram ran from a new depot in Newtown to the northern side of the Basin Reserve. Within three years, trams were running through the Hataitai Tunnel to Kilbirnie, Miramar and Seatoun, out to Island Bay, and up to Brooklyn and Karori Cemetery. At their peak, Wellington's trams covered 52 kilometres of route.
1924 — FIRST TROLLEYBUSES
Era 1: trolleybuses debut on 29 September 1924
Wellington tried its first trolleybus — an AEC 602 running from Thorndon along Hutt Road to Kaiwharawhara. Trolleybuses use overhead electric wires (like trams) but rubber tyres (like buses). But this first attempt flopped: low patronage led to closure by 30 May 1932.
1945–1964 — THE TROLLEYBUS TAKEOVER
Trams out, trolleybuses in
In 1945, WCC decided to replace all trams with trolleybuses. The second trolleybus era began on 20 June 1949 with the Roseneath route. One by one, tram routes were converted: Wadestown (1951), Karori (1954), Northland (1956), Seatoun and Miramar (1958), Aro Street, Brooklyn and Mornington (1960), Lyall Bay (1963), Island Bay (1964). On 2 May 1964, the very last tram in all of New Zealand ran its final journey (Thorndon to Newtown).
1992 — PRIVATISATION
WCC sells its transport arm to Stagecoach
The Wellington City Council privatised its transport operations, selling to Stagecoach (a UK bus company). WCC kept ownership of the overhead wire infrastructure; Stagecoach maintained it under contract. The trolleybuses kept rolling — but the clock was ticking.
2017 — THE END OF THE TROLLEYBUSES
31 October 2017: last trolleybus runs in Wellington
After a contentious consultation process, Greater Wellington Regional Council voted to close the trolleybus system entirely. The last trolleybus ran on Halloween 2017. Wellington had been the last trolleybus system in Oceania, and the last major system in any left-hand-traffic country. Fans were heartbroken. The overhead wires came down soon after.
2018 — ELECTRIC DOUBLE DECKERS ARRIVE
First electric double deckers in the Southern Hemisphere
From 15 July 2018, Wellington's first electric double-decker buses went into service — built by Kiwi Bus Builders in Tauranga using Microvast lithium-ion batteries. They were the first electric double-deckers in the entire Southern Hemisphere. Today Metlink has 43 electric double deckers, mainly on Route 1 (Johnsonville to Island Bay).
2027 & BEYOND — THE FUTURE
Bendy buses and a fully electric fleet by 2030
Five electric articulated (bendy) buses are coming for Route 2 in 2027 — the first ever in Wellington, chosen because the Karori Tunnel is too low for double deckers. Meanwhile, Metlink's goal is for all core bus routes to be fully electric by 2030, and a completely emissions-free fleet by 2035. As of 2025, 25% of the fleet is electric — up from almost zero five years ago.
⚡ Trivia Blast

Wellington's trolleybuses ran for a combined 76 years across two eras (1924–32 and 1949–2017). When they closed in 2017, Wellington was the last city in Oceania still running trolleybuses — a title it held, somewhat reluctantly, to the very end.

What's Coming

The electric revolution (for real this time)

Wellington has been electric before — the trolleybuses were zero-emission at the point of use. But this time it's batteries, not wires, which means no need for expensive overhead infrastructure.

🔋 Wellington's Electric Bus Goals

2027: First bendy buses (electric articulated vehicles) on Route 2. Five EAVs, each carrying 65% more people than a standard bus, snaking through the Karori Tunnel.

2030: All core bus routes fully electric. About one-third of all kilometres driven by Metlink buses are already electric.

2035: Entire Metlink bus fleet emissions-free. Wellington had trolleybuses for 76 years — electric buses might last even longer.

Future tunnels: A second Mount Victoria Tunnel and second Terrace Tunnel were approved in 2025, which could dramatically speed up bus journeys to the eastern suburbs.

Key Vocabulary

Tap each card to reveal the definition

Trolleybus
tap to reveal ↓
A bus that runs on rubber tyres but is powered by electricity from overhead wires (not rails). Quieter and smoother than diesel, but can't go off its wired route.
Funicular
tap to reveal ↓
A type of railway on a very steep slope where two cars are connected by a cable — as one goes up, the other comes down, counterbalancing each other. The Wellington Cable Car is a funicular.
Articulated Bus
tap to reveal ↓
A very long bus made of two sections joined by a flexible accordion joint in the middle. Can carry 65% more passengers than a standard bus, and can still navigate corners.
BEV
tap to reveal ↓
Battery Electric Vehicle. A bus (or car) powered entirely by rechargeable batteries, with no combustion engine. Wellington's new electric buses are BEVs — they recharge overnight at the depot.
Golden Mile
tap to reveal ↓
Wellington's main city bus corridor — Lambton Quay and Courtenay Place. So many buses run here that congestion is a constant challenge, which is one reason why higher-capacity buses (double deckers, bendy buses) are valuable.
Frequent Network
tap to reveal ↓
The set of Metlink routes that run at least every 15 minutes for most of the day. In Wellington this is mostly routes 1–4. The goal is you never need to check a timetable — just show up and a bus will come.
Electrification
tap to reveal ↓
The process of switching a fleet from fossil-fuel engines to electric power. Wellington is targeting 100% electric buses by 2030–2035, which would eliminate all diesel emissions from the bus fleet.
Metlink
tap to reveal ↓
The brand name for Greater Wellington Regional Council's public transport network. Metlink doesn't directly drive buses — it contracts private operators (like Tranzit/Tranzurban and NZ Bus) to run the routes.

Quiz Time

How much did you take in?

Five questions. Choose your answer and read the explanation — even if you get it right, the extra info is worth it.

01 /05
As of 2025, how many electric buses are in Wellington's Metlink fleet?
✅ Correct! 119 electric buses.
As of 2025, Metlink has 119 electric buses — that's 25% of the total fleet of 450+. The goal is for all core routes to be electric by 2030. 450 is the total fleet size (all power types combined).
02 /05
In what year did the very last tram run in Wellington — and in all of New Zealand?
✅ 1964 — 2 May 1964 to be precise.
The Thorndon to Newtown tram route was the last surviving tram route in all of New Zealand. It closed on 2 May 1964, replaced by buses. The trams were 60 years old by then, and the city had decided cars and buses were the future. A decision many now regret.
03 /05
Wellington's electric double-decker buses arrived in 2018. What record did they set?
✅ First electric double deckers in the Southern Hemisphere!
Wellington's electric double deckers — built by Kiwi Bus Builders in Tauranga — were the first of their kind anywhere in the Southern Hemisphere. They went into service on 15 July 2018, mainly on Route 1. Metlink now has 43 of them.
04 /05
Why is Route 2 (Karori) getting bendy (articulated) buses instead of double deckers?
✅ Exactly — the tunnel is too low!
The Karori Tunnel is not tall enough for a double-decker bus to fit through. So to increase capacity on the busy Route 2 corridor, Metlink chose articulated buses instead. Five electric articulated vehicles (EAVs) will arrive in 2027, each carrying 65% more people than a standard single-decker.
05 /05
Wellington's trolleybus system finally closed in 2017. What made it historically significant?
✅ The last in all of Oceania!
When Wellington's trolleybuses closed on 31 October 2017, they were the last commercially operating trolleybus system in Oceania — and the last major one in any country where traffic drives on the left. Wellington held on to trolleybuses for 76 years across two eras. Many Wellingtonians still miss them today.

Challenge problems — think it through

These don't have a single right answer. Work them out, discuss them, or look them up.

Challenge 01

A bendy bus carries 65% more passengers than a standard single-decker. If a standard bus seats and carries 70 people at capacity, how many people can the bendy bus carry? Show your working.

Hint: 65% of 70 is 45.5. Add that to 70. What do you get?
Challenge 02

Wellington's tram network covered 52 kilometres at its peak. If you tried to walk the entire tram network at 5 km/h, how long would it take? Compare that to catching a bus the same distance at an average speed of 25 km/h in city traffic.

Hint: Distance ÷ Speed = Time. Convert to hours and minutes.
Challenge 03

The Wellington Cable Car opened in 1902. How many years has it been running? It carried 425,000 people in its first year and crossed 1 million by 1912. If it's carried roughly 1.5 million passengers per year for the past 30 years, how many total trips have been made on it since opening?

This will be a very large number. Think about how you'd estimate it.
Challenge 04 — Research

The trolleybuses were closed in 2017 partly because the overhead wire infrastructure was expensive to maintain. But Wellington is now spending money to electrify the bus fleet with batteries. Was closing the trolleybuses the right decision? Find two arguments for and two arguments against, then decide what you think.

Think about: infrastructure cost, flexibility, carbon emissions, the 76-year history of the system.
