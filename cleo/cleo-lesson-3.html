⭐ The Big Idea

First Came Darkness — Then Came Light

"The universe spent its first 200 million years in total darkness. No stars. No galaxies. Just clouds of hydrogen and helium drifting in the dark. Then gravity slowly pulled those clouds together — and the very first stars ignited."

Remember from Lesson 2 — the first atoms (hydrogen and helium) formed about 380,000 years after the Big Bang. At that point, light could finally travel through space. But it wasn't starlight. It was more like a faint, even glow — a leftover warmth from the early universe. Then that glow faded, and the universe went dark.

Scientists call this period the Cosmic Dark Ages. For roughly 200 million years — a stretch of time so long it makes your head spin — the universe was just clouds of gas floating in darkness. No light. No heat. No stars. Nothing to see, and nobody to see it.

Our Sun 🌞

How did stars form from gas?

Gravity is the key. Even though hydrogen gas was spread out across the universe, it wasn't perfectly smooth — there were tiny clumps where slightly more gas had collected. Gravity pulled those clumps together. As more and more gas fell inward, the cloud got denser. The centre got hotter. And hotter. And hotter still.

When the centre of a gas cloud reaches about 10 million degrees, something incredible happens — the hydrogen atoms are squeezed together so hard they fuse (join together) and become helium atoms. This releases an enormous amount of energy — as light and heat. The star switches on.

This is happening in our Sun right now. Every second, the Sun converts about 600 million tonnes of hydrogen into helium. That's where sunlight comes from — and that's where the warmth on your face on a sunny day in Wellington comes from. You are being warmed by nuclear fusion happening 150 million kilometres away. 🌞

What's the Cosmic Microwave Background?

Remember when we said the universe glowed faintly after the first atoms formed? That glow — called the Cosmic Microwave Background, or CMB — is still out there. Right now. It fills all of space in every direction.

It's incredibly faint — your eyes can't see it. But special telescopes can detect it. And here's the extraordinary thing: when scientists look at the CMB, they are looking at the universe as it was 380,000 years after the Big Bang. It's like a baby photo of the entire universe — and it's one of the most important pieces of evidence that the Big Bang actually happened. You'll learn more about this in Lesson 4.

📚 Key Words

Words to Know

Gravity
The force that pulls things toward each other. Everything with mass has gravity — you, the Earth, the Sun, every star. The more massive something is, the stronger its gravity. Gravity is what holds planets in orbit, keeps you on the ground, pulls gas clouds into stars, and holds galaxies together. Without gravity, the universe would just be spread-out gas forever.

Nuclear Fusion
When two atomic nuclei are squeezed together so hard they join (fuse) to make a new, heavier nucleus — releasing enormous energy. This is what powers stars. The Sun fuses hydrogen into helium. This is NOT the same as nuclear power stations on Earth, which split atoms apart (that's fission). Fusion is what stars do.
tap to reveal
Galaxy
A massive collection of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter, all held together by gravity. Our galaxy is called the Milky Way and contains about 200-400 billion stars. The universe contains more than 2 trillion galaxies. That's 2,000,000,000,000 galaxies — each with hundreds of billions of stars.
tap to reveal
Cosmic Dark Ages
The period between when the universe's first glow faded (about 380,000 years after the Big Bang) and when the first stars switched on (about 200 million years). For almost 200 million years, the universe was completely dark — no stars, no light, just clouds of hydrogen gas drifting silently in the dark.
tap to reveal
Cosmic Microwave Background
Often called the CMB — the faint afterglow of the Big Bang that still fills all of space today. It's like the universe's oldest light, released 380,000 years after the Big Bang and still travelling. Special telescopes have mapped it and it looks like a faint glow covering the entire sky in every direction. It's the universe's baby photo.
tap to reveal
Supernova
When a massive star runs out of fuel and explodes — one of the most violent events in the universe. A supernova can briefly outshine an entire galaxy. But supernovas are also how the heavier elements (carbon, oxygen, iron) get spread through space. Without supernovas, there would be no planets — and no us.

🧠 Quiz Time

Test What You Know!

1. What are the Cosmic Dark Ages?
⭐ Exactly! The Cosmic Dark Ages were about 200 million years of complete darkness after the universe's first faint glow faded — before any stars existed. Just clouds of hydrogen gas drifting silently through the dark.
The Cosmic Dark Ages were the period between when the universe's first glow faded (380,000 years after the Big Bang) and when the first stars switched on (about 200 million years). Nearly 200 million years of complete darkness — no stars, no light of any kind.
2. What force pulled clouds of gas together to make stars?
⭐ Yes! Gravity is the answer. The universe wasn't perfectly smooth — some places had slightly more gas. Gravity made those clumps pull in even more gas. Over millions of years, the clumps got denser and hotter until — STAR!
Gravity did it! Even tiny differences in how much gas was in different places gave gravity something to work with. Slightly denser regions pulled in more gas. Those regions got denser still. Over millions of years, they grew hot and dense enough to ignite as stars.
3. What is nuclear fusion?
⭐ Correct! Nuclear fusion is when atoms get squished together so hard they join into a new heavier atom — and the leftover energy bursts out as light and heat. This is what the Sun is doing right now, every second. It's fusing about 600 million tonnes of hydrogen into helium per second!
Nuclear fusion is when atoms are squeezed together so hard they join (fuse) into a new heavier atom. The leftover energy comes out as light and heat — that's starlight! It's what powers every star. Splitting atoms is called fission — that's how nuclear power stations work on Earth.
4. What is the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB)?
⭐ Yes! The CMB is the leftover glow from when the universe was 380,000 years old — released when the first atoms formed and light could travel freely. It still fills all of space in every direction today. It's one of the most important pieces of evidence for the Big Bang.
The CMB is the faint leftover glow from the early universe — released 380,000 years after the Big Bang when the universe cooled enough for light to travel freely. It's still all around us, filling all of space in every direction. Telescopes can detect it, even though our eyes can't see it.
5. Why are supernovas important for life on Earth?
⭐ Brilliant! Stars create heavier elements (carbon, oxygen, iron, gold) through fusion. When a massive star explodes as a supernova, it scatters those elements through space. Those elements formed the planets — and us. Without supernovas, the universe would only have hydrogen and helium. No planets. No life.
Supernovas spread the heavier elements through space! Stars are element factories — they fuse hydrogen into helium, then helium into carbon, then carbon into heavier elements, all the way up to iron. When the star explodes, all those atoms get flung into space — where they eventually become planets and living things.
