How Do We Know? 🔭The detective work of astrophysics

"Nobody was there. No cameras. No notes. So how do we know the Big Bang happened?"

Scientists are the world's greatest detectives. They look at clues the universe left behind — and work backwards to figure out what happened 13.8 billion years ago. This lesson is about how they did it — and who did it.

↓ scroll to begin
🔭 The Big Idea

Scientists Look Backwards Through Time

"When you look at a star, you're not seeing it as it is now. You're seeing it as it was years, or thousands of years, ago — because it took that long for the light to reach you. The further away something is, the further back in time you're looking."

Light travels very fast — 300,000 kilometres per second. But space is so enormous that even at that speed, light from distant galaxies takes billions of years to reach us. This means that when astronomers look through powerful telescopes, they're literally looking back in time.

The James Webb Space Telescope — launched in 2021 — can see galaxies so far away that their light left them over 13 billion years ago. It can see the universe when it was a baby — just a few hundred million years old. This is how we know what the early universe looked like.

Three Big Clues

Scientists found three major pieces of evidence that all point to the Big Bang. Together, they're so powerful that almost every scientist in the world agrees: the Big Bang happened.

1
The Universe Is Expanding
In the 1920s, astronomer Edwin Hubble discovered that every galaxy in the universe is moving away from every other galaxy. The further away, the faster it's moving. If you run this backwards — like rewinding a film — everything comes together at one point. That point is the Big Bang.
2
The Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB)
In 1965, scientists accidentally discovered a faint glow coming equally from every direction in space. At first they thought their telescope was broken. It wasn't — they had found the leftover heat from the Big Bang itself. It still fills all of space, 13.8 billion years later. This is one of the strongest pieces of evidence for the Big Bang.
3
The Amounts of Hydrogen and Helium
The Big Bang theory predicted exactly how much hydrogen and helium the universe should contain. Scientists measured the real amounts — and they matched the prediction almost perfectly. About 75% hydrogen, 25% helium. No other explanation can account for these exact amounts.

Here's something to think about: When scientists found the CMB in 1965, they weren't even looking for it. They stumbled across the leftover glow of the Big Bang completely by accident, while trying to fix what they thought was a broken antenna. Some of the greatest discoveries in science happened by surprise. Does that change how you think about exploring and experimenting?

🌟 The Scientists Who Did It

Women Who Unlocked the Universe

Astrophysics has been shaped by extraordinary women. Here are four of them — each one discovered or proved something that changed what we know about the universe.

🌌
Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin
Astrophysicist · 1900–1979
Discovered that stars are made mostly of hydrogen and helium — not the same stuff as Earth, as everyone assumed. Her male supervisor told her she was wrong and made her remove the finding from her thesis. Twenty years later, he published the same discovery and got the credit. She is now recognised as one of the most important astrophysicists of the 20th century.
🌀
Vera Rubin
Astronomer · 1928–2016
Discovered the first strong evidence for dark matter — the mysterious invisible substance that makes up most of the mass of the universe. She noticed that galaxies were spinning in a way that only made sense if there was far more matter in them than we could see. Her work transformed cosmology. She was nominated for a Nobel Prize many times but never received one.
⭐
Henrietta Swan Leavitt
Astronomer · 1868–1921
Discovered how to measure distances to stars — something nobody knew how to do before. By studying certain types of stars called Cepheid variables, she worked out a way to calculate how far away they are. This made it possible for Edwin Hubble to discover that other galaxies existed and that the universe was expanding. Her method is still used today.
🔭
Jocelyn Bell Burnell
Astrophysicist · Born 1943
Discovered pulsars — incredibly dense, rapidly spinning dead stars that send out regular pulses of radio waves, like cosmic lighthouses. She found the first one as a graduate student in 1967. The Nobel Prize for the discovery was awarded to her male supervisor — not her. She took this with extraordinary grace and has spent her career inspiring young scientists, especially women.
📚 Key Words

Words to Know

Telescope
An instrument for collecting and focusing light (or other types of radiation) from distant objects. Space telescopes like Hubble and James Webb are above Earth's atmosphere, which lets them see much further and more clearly. The James Webb Space Telescope can see galaxies over 13 billion light years away.
tap to reveal
Light Year
The distance light travels in one year — about 9.46 trillion kilometres. Not a measure of time — a measure of distance! The nearest star to our Sun (Proxima Centauri) is 4.2 light years away. The Milky Way is about 100,000 light years across. The observable universe is about 93 billion light years across.
tap to reveal
Dark Matter
A mysterious substance that doesn't emit, absorb, or reflect light — so we can't see it. But we know it exists because of its gravitational effects on galaxies. About 27% of the universe is dark matter. Nobody knows what it's made of — it's one of the biggest unsolved mysteries in astrophysics. Maybe one day you'll figure it out!
tap to reveal
Redshift
When something moves away from you, the light it emits gets stretched to longer, redder wavelengths. This is called redshift. Edwin Hubble noticed that light from distant galaxies was redshifted — meaning they were moving away. The further away the galaxy, the more redshifted — the faster it was moving away. This proved the universe is expanding.
tap to reveal
Cosmology
The scientific study of the origin, evolution, and eventual fate of the universe. Cosmologists ask the biggest questions imaginable: How did the universe begin? What is it made of? How will it end? Astrophysicists and cosmologists work closely together. If you become an astrophysicist, this is likely part of what you'll study.
tap to reveal
Observable Universe
The part of the universe we can see — limited by how far light has had time to travel since the Big Bang. About 93 billion light years across. But the universe may be much, much larger than this — possibly infinite. We just can't see beyond the observable edge because light from there hasn't reached us yet. We may never know how big the whole universe truly is.

🧠 Quiz Time

The Final Quiz — You've Got This!

1. When you look at a star that is 100 light years away, what are you actually seeing?
⭐ Exactly right! Light from a star 100 light years away took 100 years to reach your eyes. So you're seeing it as it was a century ago. Some stars we see in the night sky might not even exist anymore — they just haven't had time to tell us yet!
When you look at a star 100 light years away, you're seeing it as it was 100 years ago! The light took that long to reach us. Some of the stars in our night sky might have already died — we just won't find out for thousands of years.
2. What did Edwin Hubble discover that helped prove the Big Bang?
⭐ Yes! Hubble noticed that light from distant galaxies was redshifted — stretched toward the red end of the spectrum, which happens when something moves away from you. Every galaxy was doing this. Rewinding that motion leads back to a single starting point — the Big Bang.
Hubble discovered that galaxies are all moving away from each other — and the further away they are, the faster they're moving. This means the universe is expanding. Run it backwards, and everything comes together at one point. That's the Big Bang.
3. What is the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) evidence for?
⭐ Correct! The CMB is the leftover warmth from when the universe was young and glowing. It fills all of space today as a very faint, even glow. It was found accidentally in 1965 and is one of the strongest pieces of evidence that the Big Bang really happened.
The CMB is the leftover heat from the Big Bang — released 380,000 years after it happened, when the universe cooled enough for light to travel freely. It's been travelling through space ever since, getting fainter and fainter. Finding it was one of the most important moments in the history of science.
4. Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin discovered that stars are made mostly of:
⭐ Yes! Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin proved that stars are mostly hydrogen and helium — not the iron and rock people assumed. This was a revolutionary discovery. Sadly, her supervisor told her she was wrong at first. She was right. This is why it matters to trust your evidence even when powerful people disagree with you.
Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin discovered that stars are made mostly of hydrogen and helium — not the same stuff as Earth. At the time, people assumed stars were made of the same materials as planets. She proved them wrong. Her supervisor initially told her she was wrong too — but she was right!
5. What is a light year?
⭐ Exactly! A light year is a distance — about 9.46 trillion kilometres. It's the distance light travels in one year. The nearest star to our Sun is 4.2 light years away. The Milky Way is 100,000 light years across. These distances are so huge that kilometres stop being useful — so we switch to light years!
A light year is a distance, not a time! It's how far light travels in one year — about 9.46 trillion kilometres. Space is so vast that normal units like kilometres become hard to work with. The nearest star is 4.2 light years away. The edge of the observable universe is 46.5 billion light years away.
