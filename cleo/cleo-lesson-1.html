Before the BangWhat was there before everything?

"If the universe began with a bang — what was there before it?"

This is one of the hardest questions in all of science. Even the world's cleverest scientists don't have a complete answer. But they have ideas — and so can you.

↓ scroll to begin
🌑 The Big Idea

Before the Universe, There Was No Before

"The Big Bang didn't just create stars and planets. It created space itself. And time itself. Which means there was no 'before' the Big Bang — because time didn't exist yet."

Imagine asking: "What is north of the North Pole?" There's no answer — because north stops at the North Pole. There's nowhere further north to go. "Before the Big Bang" is a bit like that. Time started with the Big Bang. So there was no "before."

That's really hard to imagine. That's okay. Even Albert Einstein — one of the most brilliant scientists who ever lived — said that the very beginning of the universe is where our understanding runs out.

So what DO we know?

We know that about 13.8 billion years ago, everything — all the matter, all the energy, all of space — was squashed into a point smaller than the dot at the end of this sentence. Scientists call this the singularity. It was unimaginably hot and dense. And then... it expanded. Very, very fast.

Here's something to wonder about: scientists say the universe might have started from "nothing." But what is nothing? Right now, the empty space between stars isn't truly empty — it has particles popping in and out of existence all the time. Even "nothing" turns out to be something. 🤯

Was it an explosion?

Not exactly! The name "Big Bang" is actually a bit misleading. It wasn't like a bomb going off in empty space. It was more like space itself stretching and growing — in every direction at once. Like if you drew dots on a balloon and then blew it up — every dot moves away from every other dot, but none of them is at the "centre." The universe is like that. There is no centre. It's all expanding.

Talk about this together: Can you imagine nothing? Like, really nothing — no air, no darkness, no space, no time. Close your eyes and try. What happens in your brain when you try to picture it?

There's no right answer — this is a question scientists and philosophers have argued about for thousands of years.

Words to Know

Universe
Everything that exists — every star, every planet, every galaxy, every atom, and all of space and time. The universe is all of it. Scientists think there might be other universes too, but we can't see them.

Singularity
The name for the tiny, incredibly hot and dense point that the universe began from. It was smaller than an atom but contained everything that would ever become every star, planet, and galaxy. Scientists don't fully understand what it was like inside a singularity — the normal rules of physics don't apply.
tap to reveal
The Big Bang
The moment when the singularity began expanding incredibly fast — creating space, time, energy, and eventually matter. It happened about 13.8 billion years ago. The name was actually made up by a scientist who didn't believe in it — as a joke! But the name stuck.
tap to reveal
Expanding Universe
The universe has been getting bigger ever since the Big Bang. Not because things are flying through space, but because space itself is stretching — like dots on a balloon being blown up. Every galaxy is moving away from every other galaxy right now, as you read this.
tap to reveal
Astrophysicist
A scientist who studies the universe — stars, galaxies, black holes, the Big Bang, and the laws of physics that govern them all. Astrophysicists use telescopes, maths, and computers to ask the biggest questions humans have ever asked. You want to be one of these! 🚀
tap to reveal
Theory
In science, a theory isn't just a guess — it's an explanation that has been tested many, many times and backed up by lots of evidence. The Big Bang Theory is called a theory because scientists have found so much proof for it. It doesn't mean they're not sure!

🧠 Quiz Time

Let's See What You Know!

Read each question carefully. There might be tricks!

1. What does "singularity" mean in the Big Bang?
⭐ Yes! The singularity was the starting point — smaller than an atom but containing everything. Scientists still don't fully understand what it was like inside one.
Not quite! The singularity was the tiny, incredibly hot and dense starting point — smaller than a full stop on this page, but containing all the stuff that would become every star, planet, and galaxy.
2. Was the Big Bang like a bomb exploding in empty space?
⭐ Exactly right! The Big Bang wasn't an explosion in space — it was the expansion of space itself. Think of dots on a balloon being blown up. There's no centre, and everything moves away from everything else.
Tricky one! The Big Bang wasn't really like a bomb. It was space itself stretching — like blowing up a balloon with dots on it. The dots all move away from each other, and none of them is the "centre."
3. What did the Big Bang create — besides matter?
⭐ Brilliant! The Big Bang created space and time — not just the stuff in space. This is why "before the Big Bang" doesn't quite make sense. Time itself didn't exist yet.
The Big Bang created space AND time. This is the really mind-bending bit — time didn't exist before the Big Bang. So asking what happened "before" it is like asking what's north of the North Pole. There isn't a before!
4. How old is the universe?
⭐ Yes! 13.8 billion years. For comparison — the Earth is only about 4.5 billion years old. The universe existed for over 9 billion years before our planet even formed!
The universe is about 13.8 billion years old. Our Earth is only 4.5 billion years old — which means the universe existed for over 9 billion years before Earth even formed. That's a very long time to wait for us to show up!
5. In science, what is a "theory"?
⭐ Exactly! In everyday language, "theory" can mean a guess. But in science, a theory is a well-tested explanation with lots of evidence. The Big Bang Theory has mountains of evidence supporting it — you'll learn about that in Lesson 4!
In everyday life, "theory" can mean a guess — but in science it means something much stronger. A scientific theory is backed up by lots and lots of evidence and has been tested many times. The Big Bang Theory has enormous amounts of evidence supporting it.

Your Turn to Imagine

Write or Draw: "What Is Nothing?"

This is the question that has stumped scientists, philosophers, and thinkers for thousands of years. Now it's your turn.

  • Option A — Write: In 3–5 sentences, describe what you think "nothing" is like. Is it dark? Cold? Silent? Or is it something else entirely? There's no wrong answer.
  • Option B — Draw: Draw what "nothing" looks like to you. This is harder than it sounds! How do you draw something that isn't there?
  • Option C — Both: Draw it AND write a caption explaining your picture.

When you're done — show it to your friend and see if they drew or wrote something different. Then discuss: whose "nothing" is more nothing?

A question to sit with: If the universe began from nothing... where did the nothing come from? Scientists genuinely don't know. This is a real open question in astrophysics. One day, someone will figure it out. Maybe it'll be you. 🌟
