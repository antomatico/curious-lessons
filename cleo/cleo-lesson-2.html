The First Three MinutesThe most dramatic three minutes in the history of everything

"In less time than it takes to brush your teeth — the universe was born."

In the first three minutes after the Big Bang, everything that matter is made of came into existence. No stars yet. No planets. But the building blocks for all of it — created in a flash of unimaginable heat.

💥 The Big Idea

The Universe Went From Nothing to Something in a Flash

"In the first second, the universe went from a point smaller than an atom to bigger than a galaxy. And it was so hot that normal matter couldn't even exist yet — just raw, wild energy."

Here's what happened, step by step. It all sounds impossible — but every piece of this has been tested by scientists and matched by real evidence found in the universe today.

Time: 0
(The Bang)
The singularity begins expandingThe universe is smaller than an atom. Temperature: over 1,000 trillion trillion degrees. There is no matter yet — only pure, blazing energy. Space itself is expanding faster than the speed of light.
0.000001 seconds
First particles appearAs the universe expands and cools (just a little), tiny particles called quarks form from the energy. These are the smallest building blocks we know of. They can't stay alone — they immediately start sticking together.
1 second
Protons and neutrons formQuarks stick together to make protons and neutrons — the parts at the centre of every atom in your body. It's still far too hot for atoms to exist. Temperature is now "only" about 10 billion degrees.
3 minutes
First atomic nuclei!Protons and neutrons combine to form the very first atomic nuclei — the cores of the first atoms. Mostly hydrogen (the simplest atom), with some helium. These are the two most common elements in the universe today.
380,000 years
First complete atomsAfter hundreds of thousands of years of cooling, electrons finally join the nuclei — making the universe's first complete atoms. Light can now travel freely for the first time. The universe lights up! (Well — dimly glows. Stars come later.)

Here's something wild: Every atom in your body was forged in stars — stars that were born from the hydrogen and helium made in those first three minutes. The carbon in your bones, the oxygen you breathe, the iron in your blood — all of it traces back to that first explosion. You are made of stardust from the Big Bang. 🌟

How hot was it?

The temperature at the start of the Big Bang was about 1,800 trillion trillion degrees. To put that in perspective: the surface of the sun is about 5,500 degrees. The Big Bang was roughly 300 thousand trillion trillion times hotter than that. Numbers like this don't really fit in our brains — which is part of what makes astrophysics so exciting.

Here's a question to discuss: We said "you are made of stardust." What do you think that means? Does knowing that change how you feel about yourself — or about stars? Is there a difference between "you" and "stardust"?

Astrophysicist Neil deGrasse Tyson says this is the thing that makes him feel most connected to the universe. What do you think?

📚 Key Words

Words to Know

Atom
The basic building block of all matter. Everything you can touch — your hand, a rock, water, air — is made of atoms. Atoms are incredibly tiny. A single human hair is about a million atoms wide. Atoms are made of even tinier parts: a nucleus in the middle (protons + neutrons) and electrons zipping around the outside.
tap to reveal
Proton
A tiny particle found in the nucleus (centre) of every atom. Protons have a positive electric charge. The number of protons in an atom determines what element it is — hydrogen has 1 proton, helium has 2, carbon has 6. You have billions of billions of billions of protons in your body right now.
tap to reveal
Hydrogen
The simplest and most common element in the universe — one proton, one electron. About 75% of all normal matter in the universe is hydrogen. The sun is mostly hydrogen. Stars work by squishing hydrogen atoms together to make helium, and that releases enormous amounts of energy — that's why stars shine!
tap to reveal
Helium
The second simplest element — two protons, two neutrons, two electrons. About 25% of normal matter in the universe is helium (made in the first few minutes of the Big Bang). Helium is also what makes balloons float! It's lighter than air. Your birthday balloons contain atoms that were made 13.8 billion years ago.
tap to reveal
Matter
Anything that has mass and takes up space. You are matter. This screen is matter. Air is matter. Matter is made of atoms. In the very first fraction of a second after the Big Bang, there was no matter — only energy. Matter formed as the universe cooled down. Everything you can see and touch came from that cooling.
tap to reveal
Energy
The ability to do work or cause change. Heat is energy. Light is energy. Movement is energy. One of the most important discoveries in science (by Einstein!) is that matter and energy are actually two forms of the same thing — you can convert one into the other. E = mc² is the equation that describes this.

🧠 Quiz Time

Test What You Know!

1. What two elements were made in the first three minutes of the Big Bang?
⭐ Yes! Hydrogen and helium — the two simplest elements — were made in the first few minutes. Everything else (carbon, oxygen, iron...) was made later inside stars. Fun fact: the helium in birthday balloons was made in the Big Bang!
The first two elements made were hydrogen and helium — the simplest ones. Carbon, oxygen, and everything else were made much later, inside stars. You need a star to forge heavier elements!
2. What is an atom made of?
⭐ Perfect! A nucleus in the middle made of protons and neutrons, with electrons zipping around the outside. Every single thing in the universe — including you — is made of atoms built this way.
Atoms have a nucleus in the middle (made of protons and neutrons) with electrons moving around the outside. They're unimaginably tiny — a million atoms would fit across a single human hair.
3. What was the temperature at the very start of the Big Bang?
⭐ Yes — 1,800 trillion trillion degrees! The surface of the sun is about 5,500 degrees. The Big Bang was hundreds of trillions of trillions of times hotter. Numbers like this break our brains — which is why astrophysicists use special maths.
The temperature was about 1,800 trillion trillion degrees — completely unimaginable. The sun's surface is around 5,500 degrees. The Big Bang was incomprehensibly hotter than that. This is why normal matter couldn't exist at first — it would have been instantly destroyed.
4. When was the very first complete atom formed?
⭐ Exactly right! Even though protons and neutrons formed in the first few minutes, it took 380,000 years for the universe to cool down enough for electrons to join them and make complete atoms. Before that, light couldn't even travel freely — the universe was like a thick glowing fog.
It took 380,000 years! Protons and neutrons formed in the first minutes, but the universe was still too hot for electrons to attach to them. For nearly 400,000 years, the universe was a thick, hot fog. Then things cooled enough for atoms to form — and light could finally travel.
5. What does it mean to say "you are made of stardust"?
⭐ Yes! The atoms in your body — carbon, oxygen, nitrogen — were made inside stars that lived and died billions of years before our solar system formed. When those stars exploded (as supernovas), they spread those atoms into space. Eventually that material became part of Earth — and part of you. It's literally true, not just poetic!
It's literally true! The heavy atoms in your body — carbon in your bones, oxygen in your breath, iron in your blood — were forged inside ancient stars. Those stars exploded, spreading atoms through space. Eventually that material became part of our solar system, Earth, and you. You really are made of stardust.
