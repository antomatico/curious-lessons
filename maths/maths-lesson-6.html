> The Big Idea

Cryptography Is Applied Mathematics

"Encryption takes information and scrambles it using a mathematical rule, so only someone who knows the rule (the key) can unscramble it. The strength of encryption is never secrecy about the method — it's the mathematical difficulty of reversing it without the key."

The principle is called Kerckhoffs's principle (1883): assume the enemy knows the method. Security must come entirely from the key. Modern encryption systems — including RSA and AES — are published openly. The algorithms are public knowledge. Breaking them is still computationally infeasible because the underlying mathematics is hard to reverse.

The Caesar Cipher — Where It All Started

> How a Caesar Cipher Works

METHODShift each letter by a fixed number
EXAMPLEShift = 3
A → DEach letter moves 3 places forward in the alphabet
PLAINATTACK AT DAWN
CIPHERDWWDFN DW GDZQ
WEAKNESSOnly 25 possible shifts. Try them all — one will be readable. This is called a "brute force" attack.

Julius Caesar used shift-3 to communicate with his generals. It was effective — because most of his enemies were illiterate. The cipher itself is trivially weak: there are only 25 possible keys. Any modern computer breaks it in microseconds. But it demonstrates the core idea: a mathematical transformation using a shared key.

Frequency Analysis — How Arab Mathematicians Broke Ancient Ciphers

In the 9th century, Arab polymath Al-Kindi noticed that in any language, some letters appear far more often than others. In English, 'E' appears about 13% of the time, followed by 'T', 'A', 'O'. If you count letter frequencies in a ciphertext, the most common cipher-letter is probably 'E'. This technique — frequency analysis — broke every substitution cipher for 600 years, until the Renaissance produced more sophisticated methods.

The Enigma Machine and Alan Turing

Nazi Germany's Enigma machine used a series of rotating mechanical wheels to perform a much more complex substitution — one that changed with every keypress. With three wheels, each with 26 positions, plus a plugboard, the number of possible settings was 158 quintillion. Brute force was impossible.

Alan Turing — a Cambridge mathematician — didn't try to break it by brute force. He found a mathematical weakness in how Enigma was used: operators often ended messages with predictable phrases ("HEIL HITLER", weather reports). By exploiting these "cribs" — known plaintext fragments — Turing designed the Bombe machine, which narrowed the possible settings to a manageable search space. He didn't compute all possibilities; he eliminated most of them mathematically.

Historians estimate Turing's work shortened World War II by two to four years and saved between 14 and 21 million lives. He was prosecuted by the British government for being gay in 1952, subjected to chemical castration, and died at 41. He was given a royal pardon in 2013 — 61 years after his death.

Modern Encryption: RSA and the Prime Connection

In 1977, three MIT mathematicians — Rivest, Shamir, and Adleman — published the RSA algorithm. You met prime numbers in Lesson 2. Here's how they protect your data:

> RSA — How Prime Numbers Protect Messages

STEP 1Pick two enormous prime numbers: p and q. (In real RSA, these are 1,024+ digits each)
STEP 2Multiply them: n = p × q. This is your PUBLIC key — you publish it.
STEP 3Anyone can encrypt a message using n. To decrypt, you need p and q separately.

1553
Vigenère CipherGiovan Battista Bellaso (often misattributed to Vigenère) invents a polyalphabetic cipher using a keyword. Instead of one shift, different letters use different shifts — defeating frequency analysis. Called "le chiffre indéchiffrable" (the indecipherable cipher) for 300 years.
1863
Kasiski breaks the Vigenère cipherFriedrich Kasiski shows that repeated words in plaintext create repeated patterns in ciphertext, revealing the key length. Once the key length is known, frequency analysis works on each shift. The "indecipherable" cipher falls.
1939–1945
Bletchley Park and Alan TuringThe British Government Code and Cypher School assembles mathematicians, linguists, and crossword champions at Bletchley Park, Buckinghamshire. Turing leads the mathematical attack on Enigma. Codebreaking intelligence — codenamed ULTRA — is kept secret until 1974.
1977
RSA publishedRivest, Shamir, and Adleman publish their algorithm in an MIT technical memo. They offer $100 to anyone who factors a 129-digit number they call RSA-129. It's cracked in 1994 — by a distributed computing project using 1,600 computers over 8 months.
Today
Post-quantum cryptographyQuantum computers — if they reach sufficient scale — could break RSA by factoring large numbers quickly. NIST (the US standards body) finalised the first post-quantum cryptography standards in 2024. The arms race continues.

When you connect to govt.nz or access your IRD account, the encryption protecting that connection uses mathematics descended directly from Turing's work. New Zealand also hosts submarine cables connecting Australia to the Pacific — securing those cables' traffic is a national security concern involving the same cryptographic mathematics you're studying here.

> Key Vocabulary

Know the Words

Tap to reveal. Try to define them yourself first.

Plaintext / Ciphertext
Plaintext is the original readable message. Ciphertext is the encrypted, scrambled version. Encryption converts plaintext to ciphertext. Decryption reverses it. The key controls how the conversion happens.
tap to reveal
Symmetric Encryption
Both sender and receiver use the same key to encrypt and decrypt. Fast and efficient. Problem: how do you securely share the key without someone intercepting it? The "key exchange problem" was unsolved until the 1970s. AES (Advanced Encryption Standard) is symmetric — used to encrypt data at rest.
tap to reveal
Asymmetric / Public-Key Encryption
Two different but mathematically related keys: a public key (for encrypting) and a private key (for decrypting). Anyone can encrypt with your public key; only you can decrypt with your private key. RSA is asymmetric. Solved the key exchange problem. Powers HTTPS.
tap to reveal
One-Way Function
A mathematical function that's easy to compute in one direction but computationally infeasible to reverse. Multiplying two large primes is easy. Factoring their product back is (currently) infeasible. One-way functions are the foundation of modern cryptography — the security depends entirely on the asymmetry between these two operations.
tap to reveal
Brute Force Attack
Trying every possible key until one works. Against a Caesar cipher (25 keys): trivial. Against AES-256 (2^256 possible keys): would take longer than the age of the universe even on the fastest possible computer. Security is measured by how long brute force takes — modern encryption makes it astronomical.
tap to reveal
Hash Function
A one-way function that converts any input into a fixed-length output (the hash). The same input always produces the same hash; different inputs produce wildly different hashes. Used to store passwords (websites store your hash, not your password), verify file integrity, and underpin cryptocurrencies. Cannot (in practice) be reversed.

> Test Yourself

Five Questions.

1. What is the fundamental weakness of the Caesar cipher?
Correct. With only 25 possible shifts (shift by 1 through 25), a brute force attack tries every option in order. The first readable result reveals the message. No mathematics needed beyond reading. Modern encryption has key spaces of 2^128 or 2^256 — making brute force computationally impossible.
The Caesar cipher has only 25 possible keys (shifts 1 through 25). A brute force attacker simply tries all 25 — one will produce readable plaintext. In modern cryptography, security requires key spaces so large that trying all possible keys would take longer than the universe has existed.
2. Al-Kindi's frequency analysis works because:
Yes. In English, 'E' appears ~13% of the time, 'T' ~9%, 'A' ~8%... In a Caesar or simple substitution cipher, each plaintext letter maps to the same ciphertext letter every time. So the most common ciphertext letter is almost certainly 'E'. Statistical patterns in language survive simple substitution — making them detectable.
Frequency analysis works because languages have consistent statistical patterns. 'E' appears ~13% of the time in English. Any simple substitution cipher maps E to one symbol — which will appear most often in the ciphertext. The pattern survives because each letter maps to the same symbol each time. Polyalphabetic ciphers (like Vigenère) defeat this by using multiple substitution alphabets.
3. What was Alan Turing's key insight in breaking Enigma?
Exactly. Turing didn't brute force 158 quintillion settings. He used "cribs" — known or guessed plaintext fragments — to construct logical constraints that eliminated vast swathes of the key space. The Bombe machine mechanically tested which remaining settings were consistent with those constraints. This is the key insight: using mathematical structure to reduce an infeasible problem to a feasible one.
Turing's approach was mathematical, not computational. Operators used predictable phrases ("WEATHER REPORT", "HEIL HITLER"). If you guess the plaintext for part of a message, you can construct logical constraints ruling out most key settings. The Bombe machine systematically eliminated inconsistent settings. Turing reduced 158 quintillion possibilities to a manageable search — without a digital computer.
4. Why is RSA encryption secure, even though the algorithm is publicly known?
Yes. This is Kerckhoffs's principle: security must come from the key, not the algorithm. RSA's public key is the product of two large primes. To decrypt, you need to factor it — and no algorithm can do this efficiently for keys of sufficient size. The mathematical hardness of factoring is the security foundation. Publishing the algorithm doesn't weaken it.
Kerckhoffs's principle: assume the enemy knows the algorithm. Security comes from the key. RSA's security rests on the computational difficulty of factoring large numbers. Even knowing the algorithm perfectly, an attacker who can't factor your public key cannot decrypt your messages. The algorithm's strength is mathematical, not secret.
5. What threat do quantum computers pose to current encryption?
Yes. In 1994, mathematician Peter Shor published an algorithm that, on a quantum computer, could factor a large number in polynomial time — rather than the exponential time classical computers need. This would break RSA. Sufficiently powerful quantum computers don't yet exist for this purpose. NIST published post-quantum cryptography standards in 2024 to replace RSA before they do. The mathematical arms race continues.
Shor's algorithm (1994) is a quantum algorithm for prime factorisation that runs exponentially faster than any classical algorithm. On a sufficiently large quantum computer, it would break RSA by factoring the public key in reasonable time. Current quantum computers are too small and error-prone for this. The cryptography community is proactively developing post-quantum algorithms — NIST released standards in 2024.

For the Obsessive

Break a Caesar Cipher by Hand

  1. Here is an encrypted message: WKH TXLFN EURZQ IRA MXPSV RYHU WKH ODCB GRJ
  2. Count the frequency of each letter. Which appears most often? Guess it's 'E'. What shift does that suggest?
  3. Try that shift. Does it produce readable English?
  4. Extension: Write your own encrypted message with a shift of your choice. Give it to someone else and see if they can break it using frequency analysis.

The Diffie-Hellman Key Exchange

Before RSA, the biggest problem in cryptography was this: how do two people share a secret key over an insecure channel without anyone intercepting it? In 1976, Whitfield Diffie and Martin Hellman published a solution using a mathematical concept called the discrete logarithm — as hard to reverse as prime factorisation.

  1. Search "Diffie-Hellman key exchange colours analogy" — there's a brilliant visual explanation using paint mixing.
  2. The analogy: it's easy to mix two colours together; hard to unmix them. The maths does the same thing with numbers.
  3. Think: Why does it matter that two people can agree on a shared secret without ever meeting or communicating privately? What does this enable?
