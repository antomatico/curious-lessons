Prime
Numbers

"The atoms of arithmetic — indivisible, unpredictable, and everywhere."

2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13... Every number is either prime or built from primes. Mathematicians have been obsessed with them for 2,000 years — and still can't fully predict them. They also secretly protect every bank transaction and private message in the world.

The Big Idea

Every Number Has a Prime Fingerprint

"The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic: every integer greater than 1 is either prime itself, or can be expressed as a unique product of prime numbers. Primes are the atoms of arithmetic — every number is built from them."

A prime number has exactly two factors: 1 and itself. 7 is prime (only divisible by 1 and 7). 12 is not — it's 2 × 2 × 3. Every composite number breaks down into primes in exactly one way. This is as fundamental to number theory as the periodic table is to chemistry.

What makes primes fascinating — and maddening — is their unpredictability. There's no formula that generates only primes. There's no pattern to where they appear. They seem scattered almost randomly — and yet mathematicians have discovered deep order hiding inside that apparent chaos.

The Sieve of Eratosthenes (~240 BCE)

The head librarian of the Library of Alexandria devised a method to find all primes up to any number. Start with all numbers. Cross out every multiple of 2 (except 2 itself). Then every multiple of 3. Then 5. Then 7. Whatever's left — the numbers nothing crossed out — are prime. It's elegantly simple and still used in computing today.

How Many Primes Are There?

Infinitely many. Euclid proved this around 300 BCE with a stunning argument:

Suppose there are only finitely many primes: p₁, p₂, p₃... pₙ. Multiply them all together and add 1. Call this number Q. Q is either prime itself (contradicting our assumption) or divisible by some prime — but it leaves remainder 1 when divided by any of our supposed full list of primes. Either way, a prime exists outside our list. Contradiction. Therefore infinitely many primes exist. The whole proof fits in four lines.

The Riemann Hypothesis

The deepest unsolved problem in mathematics. Bernhard Riemann proposed in 1859 that the distribution of prime numbers is controlled by the zeroes of a complex function called the Riemann Zeta Function. If true, it would tell us precisely how primes are distributed. It remains unproved. The Clay Mathematics Institute has offered a $1 million prize for a proof — unclaimed for 165 years.

Every time you connect to a website beginning with https://, your phone or computer performs a calculation involving enormous prime numbers. The padlock icon you see means two primes — each hundreds of digits long — were multiplied together to create an encryption key. Factoring that product back into its two primes would take the world's fastest supercomputer longer than the age of the universe. Your banking app, your messages, your passwords — all protected by the unpredictability of prime numbers. Euclid couldn't have imagined this application of his proof.

Key Vocabulary

Know the Words

Prime Number
A natural number greater than 1 with exactly two factors: 1 and itself. Note: 1 is NOT prime (it has only one factor). 2 is the only even prime. After 2, all primes are odd — but not all odd numbers are prime.

Composite Number
Any integer greater than 1 that is NOT prime — meaning it has at least one factor other than 1 and itself. Every composite number can be broken into prime factors in exactly one way (Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic). Example: 12 = 2 × 2 × 3.
tap to reveal
Prime Factorisation
Breaking a number down into its prime components. 360 = 2³ × 3² × 5. Every composite number has exactly one prime factorisation (ignoring order). This uniqueness — one fingerprint per number — is the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic.
tap to reveal
Mersenne Prime
A prime of the form 2ⁿ − 1. Named after French monk Marin Mersenne. Not every such number is prime (2⁴ − 1 = 15 = 3 × 5), but when they are, they tend to be record-breakingly large. All of the largest known primes are Mersenne primes.
tap to reveal
Twin Primes
Pairs of primes that differ by 2: (3,5), (5,7), (11,13), (17,19), (41,43)... The Twin Prime Conjecture says there are infinitely many such pairs. Nobody has proved this. It's one of the oldest unsolved problems in mathematics — simple to state, extraordinarily hard to resolve.
tap to reveal
RSA Encryption
A cryptographic system based on the difficulty of factoring large numbers. You publish a product of two large primes as your "public key." Only someone who knows the two primes can decode messages — and finding those primes from their product is computationally infeasible. Powers HTTPS, banking, email, and most internet security.

Test Yourself

Five Questions.

1. Is 1 a prime number?
Correct. 1 is not prime. A prime number must have exactly two distinct positive factors. 1 has only one factor (itself). Excluding 1 from the primes makes the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic work cleanly — otherwise you'd have infinite factorisations (12 = 1×2×2×3 = 1×1×2×2×3, etc.).
1 is not prime. The definition requires exactly two distinct factors. 1 only has one factor. The reason this matters: if 1 were prime, the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic (unique factorisation) would break — every number would have infinitely many factorisations by including 1 as many times as you like.
2. What is the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic?
Yes. Every integer greater than 1 has a unique prime factorisation. 12 = 2² × 3 — always, for every 12 that has ever existed. This uniqueness is why primes are called the "atoms" of arithmetic. It's why RSA encryption works — the product of two primes has exactly one factorisation.
The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic states that every integer greater than 1 is either prime or can be written as a unique product of primes. "Unique" is the key word — there's only one way to do it. 60 = 2² × 3 × 5, and no other prime factorisation exists for 60.
3. Euclid's proof that there are infinitely many primes works by:
Correct. Proof by contradiction. Assume only finitely many primes. Multiply them all and add 1. This number either is prime (we've found one outside our list) or has a prime factor — but it leaves remainder 1 when divided by each prime in our list. Either way we get a prime not in our list. Contradiction. QED. It's astonishing that this much insight fits in a paragraph.
Euclid used proof by contradiction. Suppose only finitely many primes exist: p₁, p₂... pₙ. Form Q = (p₁ × p₂ × ... × pₙ) + 1. Q is either prime itself, or has a prime factor — but dividing Q by any pᵢ leaves remainder 1, so none of our primes divides Q. Therefore a prime exists outside our supposed complete list. Contradiction.
4. Why are prime numbers used in RSA encryption?
Exactly. The asymmetry is the key. 17 × 19 = 323 — easy. But given 323, finding 17 and 19 requires trial and error. Scale this to numbers with hundreds of digits and the factoring problem becomes practically impossible even with the world's fastest computers. This one-way difficulty is what makes RSA secure.
The security comes from asymmetry: multiplying two primes is fast (milliseconds), but factoring their product back into those primes is hard — exponentially harder as the numbers grow. With primes hundreds of digits long, the best-known factoring algorithms would take longer than the age of the universe on today's fastest supercomputers.
5. What is the Twin Prime Conjecture, and why is it interesting?
Yes. Twin primes are pairs like (3,5), (11,13), (17,19), (41,43)... The conjecture says these continue forever. Despite being one of the simplest statements in number theory, it remains unproved after millennia of effort. In 2013, Yitang Zhang proved that there are infinitely many prime pairs separated by at most 70 million — a massive breakthrough — but the gap from 70 million to 2 is still the work of the future.
Twin primes are pairs differing by 2: (3,5), (5,7), (11,13), (17,19)... The conjecture says there are infinitely many. Simple to state — extraordinarily hard to prove. Nobody has managed it. Note: Goldbach's Conjecture (every even number > 2 is the sum of two primes) is the other famous unsolved prime problem.
Go Deeper

For the Obsessive

The Ulam Spiral

In 1963, mathematician Stanislaw Ulam was bored in a meeting and started writing numbers in a spiral on graph paper, then circling the primes. He discovered that they cluster along diagonal lines — far more than random chance would predict. Nobody knows why.

  1. Search "Ulam spiral" on Wikipedia — look at the image. The diagonal patterns are unmistakable.
  2. Try drawing your own on graph paper: start 1 in the middle, spiral outward, circle the primes.
  3. Think: If primes are "unpredictable," why do they show visual patterns? What does this suggest about the nature of randomness in mathematics?

Goldbach's Conjecture (1742)

Christian Goldbach wrote to Euler: "I believe every even number greater than 2 is the sum of two primes." 4=2+2, 6=3+3, 8=3+5, 100=3+97... Verified for every even number up to 4×10¹⁸. Still unproved.

  1. Verify it for the first 10 even numbers. Can you always find the pair?
  2. Is there a pattern in which prime pairs work? Do some numbers have many valid pairs?
  3. Research: What's the closest anyone has come to proving it? The "ternary Goldbach conjecture" (every odd number > 5 is the sum of three primes) was proved in 2013 — why is the binary version harder?
