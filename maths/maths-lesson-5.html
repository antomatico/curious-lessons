The Big Idea

Probability Measures Uncertainty — Not Randomness

"Probability doesn't tell you what will happen. It tells you how likely different outcomes are across many trials. The mistake people make is applying long-run statistics to single events — and then being surprised when their intuition fails."

Probability is expressed as a number between 0 (impossible) and 1 (certain). A fair coin flip is 0.5. Rolling a 6 on a die is 1/6 ≈ 0.167. The sum of all possible outcomes always equals 1.

What makes probability counterintuitive is how probabilities combine. Your brain is not naturally equipped to multiply small fractions together, or to account for the number of possible pairs in a group, or to update beliefs when new information arrives. Mathematics is.

The Birthday Problem

1
How many people do you need for a 50% chance of a shared birthday?

Most people guess 182 or 183 — about half of 365. That's the wrong question. The correct answer is just 23 people.

Why? Because you're not asking "what's the chance that someone shares MY birthday?" You're asking "what's the chance that ANY two people in the group share a birthday?" With 23 people, there are 23×22/2 = 253 possible pairs. Each pair has a 1/365 chance of sharing a birthday. The probability that NO pair shares is (364/365)^253 ≈ 49.95%. So the probability that at least one pair DOES share is about 50.05%.

Key insight

The number of pairs grows much faster than the number of people. 23 people → 253 pairs. 50 people → 1,225 pairs. The probability is driven by the number of chances, not just the number of people.

The Monty Hall Problem

2
Should you switch doors?

You're on a game show. Three doors: behind one is a car, behind two are goats. You pick door 1. The host (who knows what's behind each door) opens door 3, revealing a goat. He asks: do you want to switch to door 2?

Most people say it doesn't matter — there are two doors left, so it's 50/50. This is wrong. You should always switch. Switching wins 2/3 of the time.

Why switching wins 2/3 of the time

When you first pick, you have a 1/3 chance of being right. The host's action doesn't change your original probability — your door still has 1/3. But the other remaining door now carries all the remaining 2/3 probability. The host's knowledge is the key — if he opened randomly and might reveal the car, it would be 50/50. But he always reveals a goat, which transfers probability to the other door.

Key Vocabulary

Know the Words

Probability
A number between 0 and 1 measuring the likelihood of an event. P=0 means impossible; P=1 means certain; P=0.5 means equally likely to happen or not. All possible outcomes must sum to 1. Can also be expressed as a fraction, decimal, or percentage.
tap to reveal
Independent Events
Two events are independent if the outcome of one doesn't affect the probability of the other. Coin flips are independent — getting heads doesn't change the next flip's probability. The probability of both happening is P(A) × P(B). Many probability mistakes come from assuming dependence or independence incorrectly.
tap to reveal
Conditional Probability
The probability of event A given that event B has already occurred. Written P(A|B). The Monty Hall problem involves conditional probability — the probability of the car being behind door 2, given that the host opened door 3. Bayes' Theorem is the tool for calculating conditional probabilities.
tap to reveal
Expected Value
The average outcome you'd expect over many trials. Roll a die many times: expected value = (1+2+3+4+5+6)/6 = 3.5. You'll never roll 3.5 — but your average over many rolls will converge to it. Expected value tells you whether a bet or decision is worthwhile in the long run.
tap to reveal
Bayes' Theorem
A formula for updating probabilities when new evidence arrives: P(A|B) = P(B|A) × P(A) / P(B). Used in medical testing (what's the chance you have a disease given a positive test?), spam filtering, and machine learning. Arguably the most practically useful formula in applied mathematics.
tap to reveal
Law of Large Numbers
As the number of trials increases, the observed average gets closer to the expected value. Flip a coin 10 times — you might get 8 heads. Flip 10,000 times — you'll be very close to 50% heads. This is why casinos always win in the long run: the house edge is small, but the law of large numbers guarantees it accumulates.

Test Yourself

Five Questions. Trust the Maths, Not Your Gut.

1. You flip a fair coin 9 times and get heads every time. What is the probability of heads on the 10th flip?
Correct. Coin flips are independent events. The coin has no memory. The probability of heads is always 50%, regardless of previous results. The belief that it's "due" to be tails is called the Gambler's Fallacy — one of the most common and costly probability errors humans make.
Each coin flip is independent. The coin has no memory of past results. The probability is always exactly 50%. The belief that tails is "overdue" is the Gambler's Fallacy — and it has cost gamblers and investors enormous amounts of money throughout history. Past results don't change future probabilities for independent events.
2. In the Monty Hall problem, you should switch because:
Yes. Your original pick is 1/3 probability. The host always reveals a goat (using his knowledge). This action doesn't change your door's 1/3, but it concentrates all the remaining 2/3 probability onto the one other remaining door. So switching gives 2/3 probability of winning. This was rigorously proved — and famously contested by thousands of academics who wrote angry letters before being shown simulations proving it.
Your door was chosen before any information — it stays at 1/3. The host's action (always revealing a goat using his knowledge) doesn't change your door's probability, but it forces the remaining 2/3 probability onto the other door. Switching gives 2/3 chance of winning vs staying which gives 1/3. The key is the host's knowledge — he doesn't open doors randomly.
3. Why does the Birthday Problem give a result so much lower than most people expect?
Yes. Most people think about one person's birthday matching the group. But the question is whether ANY two people share a birthday. With 23 people there are 23 × 22 / 2 = 253 distinct pairs — 253 chances for a match. The probability grows with pairs, not people, which is why the number is so much lower than expected.
The key is pairs, not people. 23 people → 253 pairs. Each pair has a 1/365 ≈ 0.27% chance of sharing a birthday. But with 253 chances, these small probabilities accumulate. People expect ~183 (half of 365) because they're thinking about one person vs 365 days — not pair-wise comparisons. The combinatorial explosion is what drives the result.
4. What is "expected value" and why does it matter for decision-making?
Yes. Expected value = sum of (outcome × probability) for each outcome. It's the long-run average. A lottery ticket with 1/1,000,000 chance of winning $500,000 has EV = 500,000 × 0.000001 = $0.50. If it costs $2, it's a bad bet in expected value terms — even though someone wins. Casinos survive because every game has a negative expected value for players.
Expected value is the long-run average: sum of (each outcome × its probability). For a fair die: (1+2+3+4+5+6)/6 = 3.5. You'll never roll 3.5, but your average over thousands of rolls converges to it. For decision-making: a bet is "worth taking" if its expected value is positive. This underpins insurance, gambling, investing, and medical treatment decisions.
5. A medical test is 99% accurate. A disease affects 1% of the population. You test positive. What is the probability you actually have the disease?
This is Bayes' Theorem in action. In 10,000 people: 100 have the disease (1%), 9,900 don't. The test correctly identifies 99 of the 100 sick people (99% accurate). But it also gives 99 false positives (1% of 9,900). So of 198 positive tests, only 99 are real — about 50%. A 99% accurate test sounds near-perfect, but with a rare disease, half of positives are false. This is why confirmatory tests exist.
This is the base rate neglect fallacy, solved by Bayes' Theorem. In 10,000 people: 100 are sick, 9,900 healthy. Test catches 99 sick people correctly. Test gives 99 false positives (1% of 9,900). Total positives: 198. True positives: 99. Probability you're sick given positive test: 99/198 = 50%. The rare disease means false positives swamp true positives, despite the high accuracy.

Simulate the Birthday Problem

  1. Get a group of people (family, class) and check for shared birthdays. Does it match the prediction?
  2. Search "Birthday Problem calculator" online — find a tool that lets you see the exact probability for any group size.
  3. Extend it: What if there are only 30 possible birthdays? How does the calculation change? What does this tell you about how group size and sample space interact?

The Simpson's Paradox

A statistical phenomenon where a trend appears in several groups of data but disappears (or reverses) when the groups are combined. It's appeared in studies of university admissions, medical treatments, and cricket batting averages.

  1. Search "Simpson's Paradox batting average" for a cricket example.
  2. Then search "Simpson's Paradox UC Berkeley admissions" for a famous real-world case that changed how universities handle discrimination claims.
  3. Think: What does Simpson's Paradox suggest about how we should present and interpret statistics? Who might benefit from presenting data in a misleading way?
