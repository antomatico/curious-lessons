Pi & Circles

"The most famous number in mathematics — and it hides everywhere."

You've memorised some of its digits. Archimedes spent his life trying to pin it down. Ancient Egyptians used a rough version of it to build the pyramids. And today it's been calculated to over 100 trillion decimal places — and still nobody's found the end.

The Big Idea

A Number That Cannot Be Tamed

"π is the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter. Always. For every circle that has ever existed or will ever exist. It cannot be written as a fraction. It never repeats. And it appears in places that have nothing to do with circles."

Draw any circle. Measure around the outside (the circumference). Divide by the distance straight across the middle (the diameter). You will always get the same answer: 3.14159265... It doesn't matter if the circle is the size of a coin or the size of the Earth. The ratio is always π.

This is remarkable. It means something deep about the geometry of space itself. And it gets stranger: π is irrational — it cannot be written as a fraction of two whole numbers. And it's transcendental — it cannot be the solution to any algebraic equation with whole-number coefficients. It exists outside the neat world of everyday arithmetic.

The Circle Formulas

C = πd = 2πr
Circumference: the distance around the circle. d = diameter, r = radius (half the diameter)
A = πr²
Area: the space inside the circle. Why r²? Because area scales with the square of the size.
V = (4/3)πr³
Volume of a sphere. Notice π appears again — because spheres are built from circles.

The History: How Humans Chased π

~1650 BCE
Egypt
The Rhind PapyrusAncient Egyptians used a value of about 3.16 — close but not exact. Good enough to build the pyramids. They didn't know it was irrational; they were just solving practical problems.
~250 BCE
Syracuse
Archimedes and the Polygon MethodThe breakthrough. Archimedes drew a circle, then drew polygons inside and outside it — the more sides, the closer they squeezed to the circle's true circumference. Using a 96-sided polygon, he proved π lay between 3 10/71 and 3 1/7. No calculator. Pure geometry and thought.
~480 CE
China
Zu ChongzhiThe Chinese mathematician calculated π to 7 decimal places (3.1415926) — more accurate than any European would achieve for the next 900 years. He used a polygon with 24,576 sides.
1706
England
The symbol π is bornWelsh mathematician William Jones first used the Greek letter π for this ratio. Euler popularised it in 1737, and it stuck forever.
1882
Germany
Proven transcendentalFerdinand von Lindemann proved π is transcendental — settling once and for all that you cannot "square the circle" (an ancient geometric challenge). Some mathematical quests are genuinely impossible.
2022
Google
100 trillion digitsA Google engineer calculated π to 100 trillion decimal places. The computation took 157 days. Nobody expects to find anything new — it's partly a demonstration of computing power, partly pure mathematical obsession.

The meander ratio of rivers — how winding a river is compared to the straight-line distance from source to mouth — averages π worldwide. Geomorphologist Hans-Henrik Stølum discovered this in 1996. A straight river has a ratio of 1. A wildly winding one might reach π. This isn't a coincidence — it emerges from the physics of how water erodes bends. Wellington's Hutt River, which you learned about in Geography, has its own meander ratio — and it's governed by the same mathematics.

Where Else Does π Appear?

This is where it gets strange. π shows up far outside geometry:

Probability: If you drop a needle randomly on a floor with parallel lines spaced the same distance as the needle's length, the probability of it crossing a line is 2/π. This is Buffon's Needle — discovered in 1777.

Statistics: The famous bell curve (normal distribution) — which describes everything from heights to exam scores to measurement errors — contains π in its formula.

Physics: π appears in Einstein's field equations of general relativity. It appears in Heisenberg's uncertainty principle. It appears in the formula for the period of a pendulum. Circles are everywhere — and so is π.

Euler's Identity: eiπ + 1 = 0

Often called the most beautiful equation in mathematics. It connects five of the most important numbers in all of maths — e (the base of natural logarithms, ≈2.718), i (the square root of −1, an "imaginary" number), π, 1, and 0 — in a single, elegant equation. Richard Feynman called it "the most remarkable formula in mathematics." You'll understand it fully when you study complex numbers — but even now you can appreciate the mystery of why these five unrelated constants are connected at all.

Key Vocabulary

Know the Words

Tap to reveal. Try to guess first.

Irrational number
A number that cannot be written as a fraction of two integers. Its decimal expansion goes on forever without repeating. π is irrational. So is √2. Most numbers on the number line are actually irrational — rationals are the rare exception.
tap to reveal
Transcendental
A number that isn't the root of any polynomial equation with integer coefficients. All transcendental numbers are irrational, but not all irrationals are transcendental. π and e are transcendental. √2 is irrational but not transcendental (it solves x² = 2).
tap to reveal
Circumference
The distance around the outside of a circle. From Latin "circumferre" — to carry around. Always equals π × diameter. If you unrolled a circle's edge into a straight line, it would be exactly π times the circle's width.
tap to reveal
Diameter vs Radius
The diameter is the distance straight across the circle through the centre. The radius is the distance from the centre to the edge — exactly half the diameter. Most circle formulas use radius because it's measured from the natural reference point: the centre.
tap to reveal
Archimedes' Method
The technique of inscribing (drawing inside) and circumscribing (drawing outside) polygons around a circle, then computing their perimeters to squeeze tighter and tighter bounds on π. The more sides the polygon, the closer you get. This is one of the earliest uses of what we now call a "limit" — a concept central to calculus.
tap to reveal
Ratio
A relationship between two quantities — how many times one contains the other. The ratio of circumference to diameter is always π:1. Ratios are central to mathematics because they often reveal constant relationships (like π) that hold regardless of scale.

Test Yourself

Five Questions. Read Carefully.

1. Archimedes approximated π using polygons. Why did more sides give a better approximation?
Correct. As you add more sides, the polygon's shape approaches the circle more and more closely — "in the limit," an infinite-sided polygon IS a circle. Archimedes was essentially using the concept of limits 2,000 years before calculus was invented to formalise it.
More sides means the polygon approximates the circle's shape more closely, so its perimeter gets closer to the true circumference. Archimedes had polygons both inside and outside the circle, squeezing π between two bounds that got tighter as the sides increased.
2. What does it mean to say π is "irrational"?
Exactly. Irrational means it cannot be expressed as a ratio of two integers. 22/7 is a useful approximation (accurate to 2 decimal places) but is not equal to π. The decimal expansion of π goes on forever with no repeating block — which is what makes it impossible to write as a fraction.
Irrational means π cannot be written as a fraction a/b where a and b are whole numbers. 22/7 ≈ 3.142857... which repeats. π = 3.14159265... which never repeats and never ends. These are different. An irrational number's decimal expansion is infinite and non-repeating.
3. A circle has a diameter of 10cm. What is its circumference?
Yes. C = πd = π × 10 ≈ 3.14159... × 10 ≈ 31.4cm. A circle's circumference is always just over 3 times its diameter — because π ≈ 3.14.
C = πd = π × 10 ≈ 31.4cm. Since π ≈ 3.14, the circumference is about 3.14 times the diameter. Easy check: a circle with diameter 10 should have a circumference just over 30 — not 20 (too small, that's just 2×d) or 100 (way too big).
4. Why is π called "transcendental" (beyond being just irrational)?
Correct. √2 is irrational but it solves x² = 2 (an algebraic equation). π doesn't solve any such equation — it's outside the algebraic number system entirely. Proving this in 1882 settled the ancient question of "squaring the circle" — it's genuinely impossible, not just hard.
Transcendental means π doesn't satisfy any polynomial equation with integer coefficients. Compare: √2 is irrational but it solves x² − 2 = 0. π doesn't solve any such equation. This is a strictly stronger condition than being irrational, and it took until 1882 to prove it about π.
5. π appears in Buffon's Needle probability problem. What does this suggest?
Right. π keeps appearing in unexpected places because it's a fundamental property of Euclidean space — the geometry of the flat world we live in. Any time rotational symmetry or circular geometry is involved — even hidden — π tends to show up. It's not a property of circles specifically; it's a property of geometry.
π appearing in Buffon's Needle (a probability problem with no obvious circle) reveals that it's a constant of geometry itself — not just circles. It emerges wherever rotational symmetry is involved. The fact that it appears in Einstein's equations, the normal distribution, and pendulum physics suggests π is woven into the structure of space.
Go Deeper

For When the Quiz Feels Easy

Try Archimedes' Method Yourself

  1. Draw a circle of radius 1 (diameter 2). Its circumference = 2π ≈ 6.283.
  2. Draw a regular hexagon inside it. A hexagon has 6 sides each equal to the radius = 1. Perimeter = 6. So π > 3.
  3. Draw a regular hexagon outside the circle. Each side = 2/√3 ≈ 1.155. Perimeter ≈ 6.928. So π < 3.464.
  4. Now try a 12-sided polygon. Calculate each side length and tighten the bounds further.
  5. Research challenge: How many decimal places did Archimedes get with a 96-gon? What did Zu Chongzhi achieve with his 24,576-gon?

The Monte Carlo Method for estimating π

Imagine a circle inscribed in a square (just touching all four sides). If you throw darts randomly at the square, the fraction that land inside the circle approaches π/4 as you throw more darts. This is because the circle's area (πr²) divided by the square's area (4r²) = π/4.

  1. Search "Monte Carlo pi simulation" on YouTube — there are beautiful visual demonstrations.
  2. Think: Why does this work? What's the connection between random sampling and a geometric constant?
  3. Harder: This method converges very slowly. Why? And what does this tell you about when random methods are useful vs when they aren't?
