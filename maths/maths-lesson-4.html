Infinity

"Some infinities are bigger than others. This is not a metaphor. It's a theorem."

There are infinitely many whole numbers. There are infinitely many decimals between 0 and 1. But these two infinities are not the same size. Georg Cantor proved this in the 1870s — and the mathematics community at the time nearly had a breakdown about it.

The Big Idea

Infinity Is Not a Number — It's a Concept With Structure

"The set of all even numbers is infinite. The set of all integers is infinite. But they're the same size of infinite — you can match them one-to-one. The set of all real numbers is a strictly larger infinity. Cantor proved there's no way to match them up."

When we say two sets are the same size, we mean we can pair them up — one from each set — with nothing left over. This works for finite sets obviously. But Cantor extended it to infinite sets, and the results are extraordinary.

The natural numbers (1, 2, 3, 4...) are infinite. The even numbers (2, 4, 6, 8...) are also infinite. Are they the same size? Yes — because you can pair them: 1↔2, 2↔4, 3↔6, 4↔8... Every natural number pairs with exactly one even number. There's no "gap." This seems wrong — there should be twice as many natural numbers — but that intuition is built for finite sets. Infinity doesn't obey the same rules.

Hilbert's Hotel — The Most Famous Thought Experiment in Mathematics

A hotel with infinitely many rooms, all occupied

Scenario 1 — One new guest arrives

Paradox: every room is full. Solution: ask every current guest to move to the next room (room 1→2, 2→3, 3→4...). Room 1 is now empty. The hotel is still full — just differently arranged.

Scenario 2 — Infinitely many new guests arrive

Ask every current guest to move to double their room number (room 1→2, 2→4, 3→6...). All the odd rooms are now empty — infinitely many of them. The infinite bus of new guests can fill them.

Scenario 3 — Infinitely many infinite buses arrive

This still works — using prime powers to assign rooms. There are clever numbering schemes that fit countably infinite groups into a single infinity. Cantor showed all of this.

Scenario 4 — One bus arrives with ALL real numbers between 0 and 1

This does NOT work. No matter how cleverly you try to assign rooms, you will always leave real numbers unhoused. This is Cantor's diagonal proof — the real numbers are a bigger infinity than the integers.

Cantor's Diagonal Argument

The proof is elegant. Suppose you claim to have listed all real numbers between 0 and 1 — in some order. Cantor constructs a new number by taking the 1st digit of the 1st number and changing it, the 2nd digit of the 2nd number and changing it, the 3rd digit of the 3rd number... This new number differs from every number in your list in at least one place. So your list was incomplete. This works for any list — proving you can never list all real numbers. The real numbers are uncountably infinite.

~300 BCE
Aristotle rejects actual infinityAristotle distinguished between "potential infinity" (you can always count higher) and "actual infinity" (an infinite completed set). He accepted the former, rejected the latter. This view dominated for 2,000 years.
1638
Galileo's ParadoxGalileo noted that the perfect squares (1, 4, 9, 16...) can be matched one-to-one with all positive integers — even though "there should be fewer." He concluded that the concepts of "greater than" and "less than" don't apply to infinite sets. He was right. He just didn't know what to do about it.
1873–1884
Georg Cantor revolutionises mathematicsCantor developed set theory and proved that infinities come in different sizes. His contemporaries — especially Kronecker — attacked him viciously. "I don't know what predominates in Cantor's theory — philosophy or theology," said Poincaré. Cantor suffered severe depression. He died in a sanatorium in 1918.
1900
Hilbert's ProgrammeDavid Hilbert — widely considered the greatest mathematician of his era — publicly championed Cantor's work: "No one shall expel us from the paradise that Cantor has created." He proposed that Cantor's infinity hierarchy should be formalised as the foundation of all mathematics.
1931
Gödel's Incompleteness TheoremsKurt Gödel proved that in any sufficiently powerful mathematical system, there are true statements that cannot be proved within the system. This shattered Hilbert's programme — mathematics cannot be completely formalised. Some mathematical truths are forever beyond proof.

Computer science runs on infinity-adjacent concepts. Every algorithm has a complexity — how many steps it takes as the input grows toward infinity. Big-O notation (O(n), O(n²), O(log n)) describes how algorithms behave "in the limit." Understanding that some algorithms scale fundamentally differently from others — even if they're both technically infinite in steps — is exactly the kind of thinking Cantor pioneered. New Zealand's tech sector — from Xero to Rocket Lab's software — depends on engineers who understand this deeply.

Key Vocabulary

Know the Words

Countably Infinite
An infinite set is countably infinite if its elements can be matched one-to-one with the natural numbers (1, 2, 3...). The integers, rational numbers, and even algebraic numbers are countably infinite. Cantor called this cardinality ℵ₀ (aleph-null).

Uncountably Infinite
An infinite set is uncountably infinite if it cannot be matched one-to-one with the natural numbers — it's a strictly larger infinity. The real numbers are uncountably infinite. No matter how you try to list them, you always miss some. Cantor called this cardinality 𝔠 (the cardinality of the continuum).
tap to reveal
Cantor's Diagonal Argument
A proof that shows no list of real numbers between 0 and 1 can be complete. For any claimed complete list, construct a number by changing the nth digit of the nth entry. This number differs from every entry in the list — proving the list is incomplete. Therefore the real numbers are uncountable.
tap to reveal
Cardinality
The size of a set — how many elements it contains. For finite sets, this is just counting. For infinite sets, Cantor defined cardinality using bijections (one-to-one matchings). Two sets have the same cardinality if they can be perfectly matched. The hierarchy of infinite cardinalities is: ℵ₀ < ℵ₁ < ℵ₂...
tap to reveal
Continuum Hypothesis
Is there an infinity between ℵ₀ (countable) and 𝔠 (real numbers)? Cantor believed there wasn't but couldn't prove it. Gödel (1940) and Cohen (1963) together proved something extraordinary: the continuum hypothesis is independent of standard set theory — it can neither be proved nor disproved. It's undecidable.
tap to reveal
Gödel's Incompleteness
Kurt Gödel proved in 1931 that any consistent mathematical system powerful enough to include arithmetic contains true statements that cannot be proved within the system. Mathematics is, in a precise sense, incomplete. This doesn't mean maths is broken — it means no finite set of axioms can capture all mathematical truth.

Test Yourself

Five Questions.

1. Is the set of even numbers bigger than, smaller than, or the same size as the set of all positive integers?
Correct. By Cantor's definition of size (cardinality), two sets are the same size if you can match them one-to-one with nothing left over. Match 1↔2, 2↔4, 3↔6, n↔2n... Every natural number pairs with exactly one even number. Same size. This feels wrong — but finite intuitions don't apply to infinite sets.
By Cantor's definition, two infinite sets have the same cardinality if their elements can be matched one-to-one. Every natural number n matches with even number 2n — perfectly, with nothing left over. So they're the same size (both ℵ₀), even though one "feels" bigger. Infinity breaks our finite intuitions.
2. What does Hilbert's Hotel demonstrate about countable infinity?
Yes. The hotel is "full" in the sense that every room has a guest — but you can always make room for more by rearranging. Adding 1, adding ∞, adding infinitely many infinite buses — all still produce the same countable infinity. This is why ℵ₀ + 1 = ℵ₀, ℵ₀ + ℵ₀ = ℵ₀, ℵ₀ × ℵ₀ = ℵ₀. Countable infinity absorbs finite additions.
Hilbert's Hotel shows that countable infinity is "flexible" — you can always rearrange to fit more. The hotel never truly runs out of room because the natural numbers are unbounded. Any finite number of new guests, or any countably infinite number, can be accommodated. The hotel only fails when confronted with uncountable infinity.
3. What did Cantor's Diagonal Argument prove?
Correct. The argument shows that for any attempted complete list of real numbers, you can construct a real number that differs from every entry — so the list is always incomplete. No list can capture all real numbers. Therefore the real numbers are uncountably infinite — a strictly larger infinity (𝔠) than the countable infinity of integers (ℵ₀).
The diagonal argument proves that the real numbers cannot be put in a list — they're uncountably infinite. For any claimed complete list, changing the nth digit of the nth number creates a number that differs from every entry. The list is always incomplete. This means |ℝ| > |ℕ| — there are more real numbers than natural numbers.
4. What did Gödel's Incompleteness Theorems show about mathematics?
Yes. Gödel proved that in any consistent system rich enough to include basic arithmetic, there exist statements that are true but unprovable within the system. This doesn't mean mathematics is wrong — it means no finite set of axioms can capture all mathematical truth. It was one of the most shocking results in the history of thought.
Gödel showed that mathematical truth outstrips mathematical proof. In any consistent formal system powerful enough to do arithmetic, there are statements that are true but can't be proved within that system. You can extend the system to prove them — but the extended system will have its own unprovable truths. It never ends. Mathematics is irreducibly incomplete.
5. Can the following be matched one-to-one with the natural numbers? The rational numbers (all fractions a/b)
Yes! This is one of Cantor's most surprising results. You can list all fractions by arranging them in a grid (by numerator and denominator) and zigzagging through — creating a complete list. So the rationals are countably infinite (ℵ₀), same as the integers. The real numbers (including irrationals like √2 and π) are strictly bigger — uncountably infinite.
Counterintuitively, yes. Cantor showed the rationals can be listed by arranging fractions in a grid indexed by numerator and denominator, then following a diagonal path. Every fraction appears somewhere in this list — so the rationals are countably infinite (ℵ₀). The jump to uncountable infinity happens with the reals, which include all the irrationals.
Go Deeper

For the Obsessive

Try the Diagonal Argument Yourself

  1. Write down 5 decimal numbers between 0 and 1 (any ones you like).
  2. Take the 1st digit of number 1, the 2nd digit of number 2, the 3rd of number 3... Change each digit (if it's 5, make it 6; if it's 9, make it 1).
  3. Your new number differs from number 1 in position 1, number 2 in position 2, etc. It's not in your list.
  4. Insight: This works for ANY list — you can always construct a number not in it. What does this tell you about the "size" of the gap between countable and uncountable infinity?

The Banach-Tarski Paradox

Using the properties of infinity, it can be mathematically "proved" that you can decompose a solid sphere into a finite number of pieces, then reassemble those pieces — using only rotations and translations — into two spheres of the same size as the original. This is not physically possible, but mathematically valid in a system that allows the Axiom of Choice.

  2. Think: Does mathematics describe reality, or does it sometimes go beyond it? When a mathematical result seems physically impossible, is the maths wrong — or is reality wrong — or does the question not make sense?
