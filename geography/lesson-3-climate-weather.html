01
The Big Idea

The Atmosphere Is a Heat Engine

"The Sun heats the Earth unevenly — more at the equator, less at the poles. This imbalance drives everything: winds, ocean currents, rainfall patterns, seasons. Every storm, every drought, every calm summer's day is the atmosphere trying to even out that temperature difference."

The difference between weather and climate is time. Weather is the atmosphere's conditions right now — temperature, wind, rain. Climate is the long-term average of those conditions over decades. Wellington's weather changes hour to hour. Wellington's climate — consistently windy, mild, wet — has been the same for thousands of years.

How the Atmosphere Circulates

The atmosphere doesn't move randomly. It circulates in predictable bands called circulation cells:

  NORTH POLE  (Cold — air sinks, flows toward equator)
  ↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓
  60°N  ← Polar Cell → Cool, wet (UK, Canada, NZ South Island)
  ↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓↓

  SOUTH POLE (Coldest place on Earth)
↑ Global atmospheric circulation cells — NZ sits in the southern Ferrel Cell

Wellington's famous wind is not a coincidence. New Zealand sits in the "Roaring Forties" — the latitude band between 40° and 50° south, where the Ferrel Cell drives powerful westerly winds with almost nothing to block them. There's barely any landmass between New Zealand and South America at this latitude — just open ocean for thousands of kilometres. The wind builds up speed and hits Cook Strait, which funnels it even faster between the two islands. Wellington is consistently one of the windiest cities on Earth. That's pure atmospheric geography.

Weather vs Climate — The Key Distinction

🌦 Weather

Short-term. Changes daily or hourly. What you check before going outside. Can be unusual, extreme, unexpected. "It's raining today in Wellington."

🌍 Climate

Long-term average. Changes over decades. The overall pattern. Predictable and measurable. "Wellington averages 124 days of rain per year."

Key Vocabulary

Know the Words, Own the Subject

Greenhouse Effect
The natural process by which certain gases (CO₂, methane, water vapour) trap heat in the atmosphere. Without it, Earth would be -18°C. The problem is human activity is adding extra greenhouse gases, trapping too much heat.
tap to reveal
Ocean Current
Large-scale movements of seawater driven by temperature, salinity, and wind. They distribute heat around the planet — the Gulf Stream keeps Western Europe warm; without it, London would have the climate of Moscow.
tap to reveal
El Niño
A periodic warming of the central Pacific Ocean that disrupts normal weather patterns worldwide — causing droughts in some regions and floods in others. Affects New Zealand's rainfall and temperature significantly every 2–7 years.
tap to reveal
Orographic Rainfall
Rain caused by mountains forcing moist air upward. As air rises it cools and the moisture condenses as rain. The west coast of the South Island gets up to 8,000mm of rain per year from this — while Canterbury, just over the Alps, gets less than 700mm. Same country, opposite climates.
tap to reveal
Coriolis Effect
The deflection of moving air and water caused by the Earth's rotation. In the Southern Hemisphere, it deflects winds to the left; in the Northern Hemisphere, to the right. This is why cyclones spin clockwise in the Southern Hemisphere and anticlockwise in the Northern.
tap to reveal
Climate Feedback Loop
When climate change causes effects that in turn cause more climate change. Example: melting Arctic ice (white, reflective) exposes dark ocean (heat-absorbing), which warms the water, melting more ice. The change accelerates itself.

Test Yourself

Five Questions. Read Carefully.

1. Why is Wellington so windy compared to most cities?
Exactly. Wellington sits around 41°S — right in the Roaring Forties, where powerful westerly winds circle the globe with almost no landmass to break them up. Cook Strait funnels and accelerates those winds further. It's not random — it's atmospheric geography.
Wellington's wind is explained by its latitude. At ~41°S it sits in the "Roaring Forties" — a band of persistent, powerful westerly winds with thousands of kilometres of open ocean to build up speed before hitting New Zealand. Cook Strait then funnels the wind even faster.
2. What is the difference between climate and weather?
Correct. A useful way to remember it: climate is what you expect, weather is what you get. Climate is the long-term average (over 30+ years); weather is what the atmosphere is doing right now or over the coming days.
Weather is the short-term state of the atmosphere — what it's doing now or in the next few days. Climate is the long-term average pattern of weather over 30+ years. A useful shorthand: climate is what you expect; weather is what you get.
3. The west coast of NZ's South Island gets up to 8,000mm of rain per year. Canterbury (just over the Alps) gets less than 700mm. Same latitude — what causes this difference?
Yes — this is a perfect example of orographic rainfall. Moisture-laden westerly winds rise over the Southern Alps, cool, and dump their rain on the west coast. By the time the air descends on the Canterbury side, it's dry — creating what's called a rain shadow.
This is orographic rainfall and rain shadow. Westerly winds carry moisture off the Tasman Sea. The Alps force that air upward — it cools, condenses, and rains heavily on the west coast. Dry air then descends the eastern side (the rain shadow), leaving Canterbury much drier despite being only 100km away.
4. What is a climate feedback loop? Give the best example:
Exactly. The ice-albedo feedback is the classic example: white ice reflects sunlight (high albedo). Warming melts ice, exposing dark ocean (low albedo) which absorbs heat, which warms the ocean, which melts more ice. The change amplifies itself — making it very hard to stop once it starts.
A feedback loop is when the effects of climate change reinforce themselves. The clearest example: Arctic ice is white and reflective. As it melts, it exposes dark ocean water, which absorbs heat instead of reflecting it, warming the ocean more, melting more ice. The change speeds up its own cause.
5. Why does the greenhouse effect exist in the first place — and why is it normally beneficial?
Right. The greenhouse effect is natural and essential. Without it, Earth's average temperature would be -18°C — far too cold for liquid water or life as we know it. The problem isn't the greenhouse effect itself; it's that human activities are enhancing it beyond what natural systems can handle.
The greenhouse effect is natural and vital. Without it, Earth would average -18°C. Greenhouse gases (CO₂, water vapour, methane) trap solar energy and keep the planet warm enough for life. The issue is that human activity is increasing these gases, trapping too much heat and disrupting the balance.

Go Deeper

For the Curious

The 2023 Auckland and Hawke's Bay Floods. In early 2023, New Zealand experienced its worst-ever rainfall events. Auckland received a year's worth of rain in a single day. Hawke's Bay was devastated by Cyclone Gabrielle. Scientists were clear: climate change made these events more intense, because a warmer atmosphere holds more moisture. But here's the harder question — if we know climate change increases flood risk, why do councils still allow houses to be built on floodplains? Research: "New Zealand climate adaptation policy 2023".

New Zealand has a climate that varies dramatically in a small space. The West Coast of the South Island is one of the wettest places on Earth. The Mackenzie Basin — just over the Alps — is semi-arid. Northland is subtropical. Stewart Island is subantarctic. You can experience four radically different climate zones without leaving the country. This is largely because of orographic rainfall, ocean currents, and NZ's position in the southern hemisphere circulation system. Search Google Maps and look at the satellite imagery: you can literally see the difference in vegetation between west and east of the Southern Alps.

Research Challenge — Your Local Climate

  1. Go to niwa.co.nz/climate — New Zealand's national climate science organisation (NIWA).
  2. Find Wellington's climate data. What is the average annual rainfall? Average temperature? Windiest month?
  3. Compare Wellington's climate to one other NZ city of your choice. Why are they different? Use what you've learned about atmospheric circulation and orographic rainfall to explain it.

A question to sit with: We talk about climate change as if it's a future problem. But Wellington's sea level has risen measurably since 1900, and New Zealand's glaciers have lost most of their volume in 150 years. At the same time, some politicians argue the costs of action are too high. Who decides how much future risk is worth present cost — and how do you make that decision fairly when future generations can't vote?
