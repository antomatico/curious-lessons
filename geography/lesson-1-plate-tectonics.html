01
The Big Idea

The Earth Is Not Solid All the Way Through

"The ground feels permanent. It isn't. The surface of the Earth is broken into about 20 giant pieces — called tectonic plates — and they're constantly moving, colliding, and pulling apart. Everything dramatic on Earth's surface happens because of this."

The Earth has four layers: a thin crust on the outside (that's what you walk on), a thick mantle made of rock so hot it can slowly flow like very thick toffee, a liquid outer core, and a solid inner core at the centre.

The tectonic plates sit on top of the mantle. The heat from the core creates slow currents in the mantle — like the convection currents in a pot of hot soup — and these currents drag the plates around. It's incredibly slow (about the speed your fingernails grow), but over millions of years, it moves continents.

The Three Types of Plate Boundary

Where plates meet, things get interesting. There are three types of collision:

              ↑
        New crust forms
        (Mid-ocean ridges, rift valleys)

              ↓
        One plate sinks (subduction)

  TRANSFORM (Sliding Past Each Other)

        Earthquakes along fault lines

New Zealand sits right on a convergent boundary between the Australian Plate and the Pacific Plate. Off the east coast of the North Island, the Pacific Plate is subducting (diving underneath) the Australian Plate. This is directly responsible for the Taupo Volcanic Zone — one of the most geologically active regions on Earth — and why New Zealand has so many earthquakes.

Key Vocabulary

Know the Words, Own the Subject

Click each card to reveal the definition. Try to guess it first.

Tectonic Plate
A large, rigid slab of Earth's lithosphere (crust + upper mantle) that floats on the semi-molten mantle below. There are about 20 major plates.

Subduction
When one tectonic plate is forced under another and sinks into the mantle. The sinking plate melts, and the resulting magma can rise to create volcanoes.

Magma vs Lava
Magma is molten rock underground. The moment it erupts and reaches the surface, it becomes lava. Same stuff, different location.

Ring of Fire
A horseshoe-shaped zone around the Pacific Ocean with 75% of the world's volcanoes and 90% of all earthquakes. New Zealand sits at the southern end of it.

Seismic Wave
Vibrations that travel through the Earth caused by an earthquake or explosion. Seismologists use these waves to study the Earth's interior — they can't dig there, so they listen instead.

Alpine Fault
A major fault line running 600km along the South Island of New Zealand. It moves about 20–30mm per year and is overdue for a major earthquake (Magnitude 8+). Scientists call the coming event "The Big One."

04
Test Yourself

Five Questions. No Guessing Allowed.

Read each question properly before answering. Some have trick elements.

1. What drives tectonic plate movement?
Correct. Heat from the Earth's core creates slow convection currents in the semi-molten mantle, dragging the plates around like items on a very slow conveyor belt.
Not quite. The moon does affect tides, but plate movement is driven by convection currents in the mantle — heat from the core causes the mantle to circulate slowly, pulling the plates with it.
2. New Zealand is on the Ring of Fire. What percentage of the world's earthquakes happen in this zone?
Yes — roughly 90% of all earthquakes on Earth occur along the Ring of Fire, because it's a chain of convergent plate boundaries all the way around the Pacific.
The correct answer is about 90%. The Ring of Fire traces the edge of the Pacific Plate and several others — nearly all the world's subduction zones are here, which is why so many earthquakes occur along it.
3. What is the difference between magma and lava?
Exactly right. It's the same molten rock — just a different name depending on where it is. Underground: magma. Once it reaches the surface: lava.
Actually, magma and lava are the same material. The difference is location — magma is underground, and lava is what we call it once it erupts and reaches the surface.
4. The Alpine Fault runs through the South Island. What type of plate boundary is it?
Correct. The Alpine Fault is a transform boundary where the Australian and Pacific plates slide past each other. Unlike the subduction happening off the North Island, here the movement is mostly horizontal — which still generates massive earthquakes.
The Alpine Fault is a transform boundary. The Australian and Pacific Plates slide horizontally past each other — similar to California's San Andreas Fault. It moves about 20–30mm per year and is overdue for a major quake.
5. If geologists can't dig to the Earth's core, how do they know what's down there?
Right. Seismic waves from earthquakes travel through the Earth and change speed or direction depending on the material they pass through. By recording these waves at different points on the surface, geologists can "see" the interior — like an X-ray but using sound.
The answer is seismic waves. Earthquakes send waves through the Earth, and by tracking how those waves change speed and direction, geologists can determine what materials they're passing through. It's effectively an X-ray of the planet.
05
Go Deeper

For the Curious

If the quiz felt too easy, here's where it gets harder.

The Taupo Supervolcano. Lake Taupo in the middle of the North Island is one of the most volcanically active places on Earth. The lake itself sits inside a caldera — the collapsed remains of an enormous eruption. The last major eruption, around 232 AD, was so violent it turned sunsets red in Rome and China. Scientists estimate it ejected 120 cubic kilometres of material into the atmosphere in under an hour. Taupo is considered a supervolcano — and it's still active. GNS Science monitors it constantly. Look up: "Taupo Volcanic Zone" on the GNS website.

Go to geonet.org.nz — New Zealand's real-time earthquake monitoring site. You can see every earthquake that's happened in NZ in the last 24 hours, right now. Look at the map. Notice where the dots cluster. That's the plate boundary.

Research Challenge — "The Big One"

The Alpine Fault is considered one of the most dangerous faults in the world because it hasn't ruptured in about 300 years — and the average interval between major ruptures is around 300 years. Scientists have a term for this: overdue.

  1. Search: "Alpine Fault Big One New Zealand"
  2. Find out: What magnitude earthquake do scientists expect? What would the impact be on the South Island?
  3. Write three sentences: What is likely to happen, where, and why can't they predict exactly when?

A bigger question to sit with: Volcanic eruptions seem purely destructive — but without them, life on Earth might not exist. How? Volcanoes released the gases that formed Earth's early atmosphere. They created the land that organisms colonised. They still cycle nutrients through ecosystems. Whakaari / White Island, despite its dangers, supports unique microbial life that scientists study to understand the origins of life. What does that tell you about how we decide whether something is "good" or "bad" in geography?
