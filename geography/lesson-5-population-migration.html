Population

Eight billion people. Every one of them living somewhere for a reason. Population geography asks why people are where they are — and what happens when they move. New Zealand's entire story is a story of migration.

01
The Big Idea

People Are Not Spread Evenly — and That's Not an Accident

"Population distribution — who lives where — is a direct result of geography, history, and economics. Nobody chose to be born where they were. And when people move, they're almost always responding to forces bigger than themselves."

About 90% of the world's population lives in just 10% of its land area. People cluster near rivers, coasts, fertile plains, and mild climates. Vast areas — the Sahara, the Siberian interior, Antarctica — are almost empty. This isn't random. It's the result of thousands of years of people going where survival was possible and prosperity was likely.

Migration — the movement of people — is as old as humanity. Humans walked out of Africa 70,000 years ago and eventually populated every continent. Today, migration continues for the same fundamental reasons: to escape danger, to find opportunity, to join family, to survive.

Push and Pull Factors

Geographers explain migration using push-pull theory — the factors that push people away from where they are, and the factors that pull them toward somewhere new.

🔴 Push Factors (Drive People Away)
  • War & conflict
  • Poverty & unemployment
  • Natural disasters
  • Famine & drought
  • Political persecution
  • Climate change
→
🟢 Pull Factors (Attract People)
  • Economic opportunity
  • Safety & stability
  • Family connections
  • Better healthcare/education
  • Political freedom
  • Climate & lifestyle

New Zealand's entire population story is migration. Māori arrived from Polynesia around 1250–1300 CE — the last major landmass on Earth to be settled by humans, navigating thousands of kilometres of open ocean using stars, currents, and swells. Then came British colonisation from the 1840s. Then waves of Chinese migrants during the gold rush, Irish famine survivors, Scottish settlers in Otago, Dalmatian gumdiggers in Northland. Post-WWII, Polynesian migration brought Samoan, Tongan, and Cook Island communities. Recent decades have brought large Asian and South Asian populations. Walk down any Wellington street and you're walking through layers of migration geography.

Key Vocabulary

Know the Words, Own the Subject

Population Density
The number of people per square kilometre. Bangladesh has ~1,100 people/km². Australia has ~3 people/km². New Zealand has ~18 people/km² — relatively sparse, with most people clustered in a few cities. Density shapes everything: services, infrastructure, housing costs, culture.
tap to reveal
Refugee
A person who has been forced to leave their country due to war, persecution, or disaster. Legally distinct from a migrant (who chooses to move). Under international law, countries are obligated not to return refugees to places where they face serious harm.
tap to reveal
Demographic Transition
The historical shift from high birth rates + high death rates (pre-industrial) to low birth rates + low death rates (developed countries). Most wealthy countries are now in "Stage 4" — ageing populations, low growth. Some are in "Stage 5" — shrinking populations (Japan, Germany).
tap to reveal
Diaspora
A population scattered across many countries from a common origin. The Māori diaspora (NZ Māori living in Australia) now numbers over 200,000. The Pacific diaspora in Auckland. The Indian diaspora globally. Diasporas maintain cultural connections across geography.
tap to reveal
Brain Drain
When educated, skilled workers emigrate from a country, taking their skills and tax base elsewhere. NZ has a significant "brain drain" to Australia — attracted by higher wages. But NZ also benefits from brain gain from other countries. Net migration shapes national economies.
tap to reveal
Climate Refugee
People displaced by climate change effects — sea level rise, drought, extreme weather. The Pacific Islands face existential threats: Tuvalu and Kiribati may be uninhabitable within decades. NZ has a special obligation here as a Pacific neighbour and significant greenhouse gas emitter.

04
Test Yourself

Five Questions. Read Carefully.

1. What is the difference between a refugee and an economic migrant?
Correct. The distinction matters legally and morally. Refugees are protected under international law because they cannot safely return home. Economic migrants choose to move and generally have less legal protection if they're deported. The distinction shapes how countries respond to different groups of people arriving at their borders.
The key distinction is choice vs compulsion. A refugee has no safe option to stay — they're fleeing war, persecution, or disaster. An economic migrant makes a choice to move seeking better opportunities. This distinction determines legal rights and obligations under international law.
2. Māori were the last people to settle a major landmass. Why did they reach New Zealand last?
Correct. Humans spread outward from Africa and Asia over tens of thousands of years. The Pacific Islands were settled progressively — closer ones first, then further ones as navigation skills improved. New Zealand, at the southern tip of the Pacific, was simply the last major habitable landmass that could be reached. Polynesian navigators were extraordinarily skilled, using stars, ocean swells, and bird patterns to navigate thousands of kilometres.
It's purely geographic distance and sequence. Humans spread across the Pacific progressively — settling closer islands first over thousands of years, developing navigation skills as they went. New Zealand is at the far southern end of the Pacific, and Polynesian navigators only reached it around 1250–1300 CE — the end of a 70,000-year human migration story from Africa.
3. What is "brain drain" and why might it be a problem for New Zealand?
Yes. New Zealand has a persistent brain drain to Australia — where wages are higher, particularly in medicine, engineering, and technology. NZ invests in educating these people, then loses their tax contributions and skills to Australia. NZ compensates partly through "brain gain" — attracting skilled migrants from other countries — but the net effect is complex.
Brain drain is when a country's educated and skilled workers emigrate, taking their earning potential and tax contributions elsewhere. For NZ, Australia is the primary destination — higher wages attract NZ-trained doctors, engineers, and IT workers. The government effectively subsidises their education, then loses the return on that investment.
4. Why might Pacific Island nations face becoming "climate refugees" in the coming decades?
Correct. Nations like Tuvalu, Kiribati, and the Marshall Islands average only 2 metres above sea level. Sea levels are rising, storm surges are intensifying, and saltwater is already contaminating freshwater supplies and agricultural land. Some communities have already begun relocating. New Zealand has negotiated a specific arrangement with Tuvalu allowing its citizens to migrate here as sea levels rise.
Many Pacific Island nations sit only 1–3 metres above sea level. As glaciers melt and ocean water thermally expands, sea levels are rising. Storm surges already flood previously safe areas. Freshwater is being contaminated by saltwater intrusion. For some communities, permanent relocation is not a distant possibility — it's already underway.
5. What is a diaspora — and why does it matter geographically?
Correct. Diasporas are geographically fascinating because they challenge the idea that culture is tied to place. The Samoan diaspora in South Auckland is larger than the population of Samoa itself. These communities send remittances (money) home, shape politics in both countries, and maintain language and culture far from their origin. Geography and identity become complicated and rich.
A diaspora is a population that has dispersed from a homeland but maintains shared identity, culture, and connection across that dispersal. The Pacific diaspora in Auckland, the Indian diaspora globally, the Jewish diaspora across centuries — all maintain cultural and economic ties across huge geographic distances. It shows that community doesn't require geographic proximity.
05
Go Deeper

For the Curious

The Dawn Raids. In the 1970s, New Zealand police conducted raids on Pacific Island communities — arriving before dawn to check whether people were overstaying their visas. The raids were widely condemned as racist: European overstayers were rarely targeted, while Pacific people were. In 2021, the NZ Government officially apologised for the Dawn Raids. Read about them at the National Library Schools collection — it's an important part of NZ's migration history that connects human geography to politics and race.

New Zealand has made a historic commitment to Tuvalu. Under the PACER Plus framework and a 2023 bilateral agreement, Tuvalu citizens have been offered a pathway to migrate to New Zealand as their islands become uninhabitable due to sea level rise. NZ is the first country to formally offer this to climate refugees. This is an extraordinary moment in migration geography — where climate science, international law, and human geography intersect directly. Search: "New Zealand Tuvalu climate migration agreement."

Research Challenge — Your Own Family's Migration Story

  1. Ask someone in your family: where did your grandparents or great-grandparents come from? When did they come to New Zealand, and why?
  2. Map the migration on a rough sketch — origin country, route if known, destination in NZ.
  3. Identify the push and pull factors that drove that migration. Were they economic? Political? Family-based? Forced?
  4. Write three sentences connecting your family's migration to the push-pull theory you learned in this lesson.

A question to sit with: New Zealand has one of the world's most generous immigration systems relative to its size. But immigration is also politically controversial — debates about housing, wages, cultural change, and national identity recur every election cycle. If you were designing NZ's immigration policy, what would you prioritise — economic need, family connection, humanitarian obligation, or cultural fit? And who gets to decide what "cultural fit" means?
