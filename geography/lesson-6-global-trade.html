The Big Idea

Resources Are Not Spread Evenly — and That Shapes Everything

"No country has everything it needs. Geography determines who has oil, who has farmland, who has minerals, who has labour. Trade is the mechanism that connects them — and whoever controls the most critical resources tends to control the most political power."

The global economy is essentially a massive system for moving stuff from where it is to where it's needed. Raw materials are extracted in one country, processed in another, manufactured into products in a third, and consumed in a fourth. This is called a global supply chain — and COVID-19 showed the world just how fragile it can be when one link breaks.

Resources That Shape World Geography

🛢️
Oil & Gas
Concentrated in the Middle East, Russia, and Venezuela. Powers wars, alliances, and entire economies. Saudi Arabia has 17% of global reserves.
🌾
Food & Agriculture
The US, Brazil, and Ukraine feed much of the world. Ukraine's 2022 invasion by Russia triggered a global food price crisis affecting 50+ countries.
⚙️
Critical Minerals
Lithium (batteries), cobalt (electronics), and rare earth elements. Congo has 70% of world cobalt. China controls 60% of rare earth processing.
💻
Semiconductors
Taiwan makes ~90% of the world's most advanced microchips. Every smartphone, car, and weapon system depends on them — making Taiwan one of the most geopolitically significant islands on Earth.

New Zealand's economy is almost entirely built on primary resource exports — things that come out of the ground or off the land. Dairy (largest export), meat, wool, timber, and increasingly tourism. NZ exports to China, Australia, the US, Japan, and the EU. This makes NZ deeply vulnerable to global commodity prices and trade relationships — particularly with China, which is now NZ's largest trading partner. When China and Australia had a trade dispute in 2020, NZ watched carefully. Small countries near powerful ones always have complicated trade geography.

How Global Trade Flows Work

Most of the world's goods travel by ship. About 90% of global trade by volume moves on container ships — enormous vessels that can carry 20,000+ shipping containers. The key strategic bottlenecks are:

Key Vocabulary

Know the Words, Own the Subject

Supply Chain
The entire sequence of steps needed to produce and deliver a product — from raw material extraction through manufacturing, packaging, shipping, and retail. Modern supply chains span dozens of countries. The iPhone involves components from 43 countries.
tap to reveal
Comparative Advantage
The economic principle that countries should specialise in producing what they're relatively best at, then trade for everything else. NZ has a comparative advantage in dairy and meat — not because no other country can produce them, but because NZ's geography and climate makes it unusually efficient.
tap to reveal
Commodity
A raw material or agricultural product that can be bought and sold — oil, wheat, iron ore, milk powder, timber. Commodity prices fluctuate based on global supply and demand and heavily affect the economies of countries that export them (like New Zealand).
tap to reveal
Trade Deficit / Surplus
When a country imports more than it exports (deficit) or exports more than it imports (surplus). NZ often runs a trade deficit — we import more goods by value than we export. China runs the world's largest trade surplus — it sells far more than it buys.
tap to reveal
Geopolitics
How geography influences politics and international relations. Countries go to war over trade routes, resources, and strategic geography. The South China Sea dispute is partly about fishing rights, partly about undersea oil and gas, and partly about who controls the world's most important shipping lane.
tap to reveal
Resource Curse
The paradox where countries rich in natural resources (oil, diamonds, minerals) often have worse economic development and more conflict than countries without them. Oil wealth in Nigeria or diamonds in Sierra Leone have funded wars, corruption, and inequality rather than prosperity. Why? Because resource wealth concentrates power without requiring broad economic development.

Test Yourself

Five Questions. Read Carefully.

1. About what percentage of global trade by volume travels by sea?
Correct — roughly 90% of global trade by volume moves by sea. This is why maritime geography — chokepoints, shipping lanes, ports — is so strategically critical. When the Ever Given got stuck in the Suez Canal in 2021 for six days, it held up an estimated $9 billion of goods per day.
About 90% of global trade by volume moves by sea. This is why naval power and maritime geography have always been central to geopolitics — controlling key shipping routes means controlling global commerce. The Suez Canal, Panama Canal, and Strait of Hormuz are not just geography; they're power.
2. What is the "resource curse" — and which of these is the best example of it?
Yes — Nigeria is the classic resource curse example. Despite being Africa's largest oil producer, the majority of its population lives in poverty. Oil wealth concentrated power among a small elite, funded corruption, enabled conflict, and actually discouraged the broader economic development that countries without resources were forced to pursue. Norway is often given as the counter-example — it managed oil wealth through a sovereign wealth fund that benefited all citizens.
The resource curse refers to countries rich in resources but poor in development outcomes. Nigeria is the textbook example — massive oil wealth, persistent poverty and instability. Norway is the counter-example: oil wealth managed through a sovereign wealth fund that now holds over $1 trillion for all Norwegian citizens. The difference is governance and institutional quality, not geography alone.
3. New Zealand's largest trading partner is China. Why might this create geographic and political risk?
Correct. When one country is dependent on a single trading partner for a large share of its exports, political disagreements can become economic weapons. China applied trade pressure on Australia in 2020 (blocking wine, barley, beef, coal imports) after Australia called for a COVID inquiry. NZ watched this closely — too much dependence creates geopolitical vulnerability.
Trade concentration is a geographic risk. When NZ exports a large share of its goods to China, it becomes vulnerable to Chinese political decisions. If China decides to restrict NZ imports (as it did with Australian goods in 2020), NZ's economy suffers. Diversifying trading partners is a geographic-economic strategy to reduce that risk.
4. Taiwan produces about 90% of the world's most advanced microchips. Why does this make Taiwan geopolitically important far beyond its size?
Exactly. TSMC (Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company) makes the chips in your phone, your car, your laptop, and most military equipment. If Taiwan were seized or its semiconductor fabs destroyed, global industry would grind to a halt within months. This is what geographers call "critical infrastructure geography" — small places with enormous global leverage.
Taiwan's leverage comes from semiconductor geography. TSMC makes chips that go into virtually every advanced piece of technology on Earth. No chips means no smartphones, no modern cars, no military systems. This gives a small island extraordinary geopolitical weight — it's not about size or population, but about what's produced there and how irreplaceable it is.
5. When the Ever Given container ship blocked the Suez Canal for six days in 2021, shops in Europe and North America faced shortages of goods. What does this reveal about global supply chains?
Correct. Modern supply chains are built on "just-in-time" principles — meaning goods are delivered exactly when needed with minimal stockpiles. This is efficient in normal conditions, but creates extreme fragility. Six days of blockage cascaded into weeks of delays, empty shelves, and billions in losses. COVID showed the same thing on a global scale: efficiency and resilience are often opposites.
The Ever Given revealed "just-in-time" fragility. Modern supply chains hold minimal buffer stock — goods arrive exactly when needed. This is hyper-efficient but fragile: one disruption at a chokepoint cascades through the entire system. Six days became weeks of delays. COVID made the same point at global scale: supply chains optimised for efficiency are fragile under stress.

Go Deeper

For the Curious

The Phone in Your Pocket. A modern smartphone contains about 60 different elements from the periodic table. The tungsten in your phone's vibration motor likely came from a mine in Rwanda. The cobalt in the battery probably from the Democratic Republic of Congo — where mining conditions are often extremely dangerous. The screen's rare earth elements processed in China. Assembled in China or Vietnam. Designed in the US. Sold globally. Your phone is a geography lesson you hold in your hand. Search: "smartphone supply chain map" and trace it.

New Zealand's economy depends on commodities — and commodity prices are set globally, not by NZ. When the global price of milk powder drops (as it did sharply in 2015), NZ dairy farmers suffer regardless of how efficiently they operate. When China restricts beef imports, NZ exporters scramble. This vulnerability is the defining geographic challenge of small commodity-exporting economies. Research: "New Zealand export partners 2024" — see how concentrated NZ's trade relationships are, and think about the risk that creates.

Research Challenge — Trace One Object

Pick one object in your house: a shoe, a piece of clothing, a piece of fruit, or your school bag.

  1. Find out where it was made (check the label).
  2. Research: what raw materials does it contain, and where do they typically come from?
  3. Identify at least three countries involved in getting that object to your home.
  4. Write three sentences describing the supply chain — and one sentence on what would happen if one country in that chain had a crisis.

A question to sit with: Global trade has lifted hundreds of millions of people out of poverty by connecting labour in developing countries to wealthy consumers. But it has also created inequality — the people mining cobalt in Congo earn a fraction of the profit captured by the companies selling the final product. Who should decide whether global trade is "fair"? Is it enough that trade creates economic growth — or does it matter how that growth is distributed? And what geography would need to change for the answer to be different?
