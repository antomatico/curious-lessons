Urbanisation& City Design

More than half the world's population now lives in cities. By 2050, it'll be 70%. Cities don't just happen — they're shaped by geography, economics, and human decisions. And those decisions determine whether cities work or fail.

The Big Idea

Cities Grow Where Geography Allows — and Fail Where It Doesn't

"No city was built randomly. Every major city on Earth sits where it does for reasons — a sheltered harbour, a river crossing, a fertile valley, a trade route. Geography didn't just influence where cities formed. It determined it."

Urbanisation is the process by which populations move from rural areas to cities. It's the defining human geographic trend of the last 200 years — and it's still accelerating fastest in Africa and Asia, where cities like Lagos, Dhaka, and Jakarta are growing by thousands of people every day.

Why Do Cities Form Where They Do?

Look at any major city and you'll find a geographic explanation:

London — the lowest bridging point on the Thames river, making it the obvious crossing and trading point. New York — a natural harbour on the east coast, ideally placed for trans-Atlantic trade. Singapore — a island at the tip of the Malay Peninsula, sitting astride the world's busiest shipping lane. Wellington — a sheltered harbour at the narrow tip of the North Island, and the natural gateway between the two main islands.

Wellington is a fascinating city geographically. It was chosen as the capital in 1865 partly because of its central location between the North and South Islands — but it sits on unstable earthquake-prone land, on a very hilly and constrained site, and in a natural wind funnel. It's expensive to build on, hard to expand, and regularly shaken. Yet it works. Why? Because geography created the problem and the solution simultaneously — the same harbour that limits expansion also provides the port, the commercial identity, and the reason people settled there in the first place.

Key Vocabulary

Know the Words, Own the Subject

Urbanisation
The increasing proportion of a population living in urban (city) areas. Happens when people move from rural areas to cities seeking work, services, and opportunity. Currently fastest in sub-Saharan Africa and South/Southeast Asia.
tap to reveal
Megacity
A city with a population over 10 million. There are currently about 35 megacities globally. In 1950 there were only two: New York and Tokyo. The rapid growth of megacities — especially in developing countries — is one of the defining geographic trends of our era.
tap to reveal
Urban Sprawl
The uncontrolled spread of a city outward into surrounding rural land, typically low-density housing dependent on cars. Leads to long commutes, loss of farmland, higher infrastructure costs, and reduced community. Auckland is a classic New Zealand example.
tap to reveal
Gentrification
When wealthier residents move into a historically poorer neighbourhood, raising property values and displacing the original residents. Can improve infrastructure but often at the cost of community and affordable housing. Happening in Wellington's Newtown and Thorndon areas.
tap to reveal
Informal Settlement
Also called a slum or shantytown — areas of cities where people build housing without official permission, often lacking clean water, sanitation, and legal tenure. About 1 billion people globally live in informal settlements. They form because cities grow faster than housing can be built.
tap to reveal
Liveability
How well a city supports the quality of life of its residents — measured by access to green space, public transport, housing affordability, safety, healthcare, and culture. Wellington consistently ranks in global top-20 liveability indexes, despite (or because of?) its compact size.

Test Yourself

Five Questions. Read Carefully.

1. Why did Wellington become New Zealand's capital city rather than Auckland, which is larger?
Correct. Wellington was chosen as capital in 1865 largely because of its geography — a central location between the North and South Islands, accessible to both. Auckland was considered too far north to serve the whole country. Geography determined the political decision.
Geography made the decision. Wellington's central position between the two main islands made it accessible to both — Auckland was seen as too far north. This is a clear example of how geography shapes political decisions, not just where people live.
2. What is urban sprawl, and why is it considered a geographic problem?
Yes. Urban sprawl typically means low-density suburbs spreading outward with no clear plan. It's a geographic problem because it destroys agricultural land, demands expensive infrastructure (roads, pipes, power) spread over a large area, and forces residents to drive everywhere — increasing emissions and commute times. Auckland is NZ's clearest example.
Urban sprawl is the outward spread of low-density suburbs, usually car-dependent. It's a geographic problem because it consumes surrounding farmland, increases infrastructure costs (pipes, roads, power lines over larger areas), and creates long commutes. Auckland sprawls significantly compared to compact Wellington.
3. About 1 billion people globally live in informal settlements (slums). What is the primary geographic reason these exist?
Correct. The fundamental issue is a speed mismatch: people are moving to cities — often from rural areas seeking work — faster than cities can build formal housing for them. So they build their own, on whatever land they can access. This is a geographic and economic problem, not a lifestyle choice.
Informal settlements form because rapid urbanisation outpaces the ability of cities (and governments) to build formal housing. Millions of people move to cities annually seeking economic opportunities, but formal housing isn't built fast enough or affordably enough, so people build their own on available land.
4. Bogotá, Colombia transformed itself in the 1990s–2000s under Mayor Enrique Peñalosa. What was the core idea behind its transformation?
Yes. Peñalosa's key insight was that a city designed for cars is a city designed for the wealthy minority who own cars. By building world-class cycling infrastructure, the TransMilenio bus rapid transit system, and reclaiming roads as public space, Bogotá made mobility democratic — accessible to everyone regardless of income.
Bogotá's transformation was about putting people before cars. Peñalosa argued that a city full of cars serves only those who can afford them. He invested in cycling paths, high-quality public transit (TransMilenio), and public parks — redesigning the city around human movement rather than vehicle movement.
5. Wellington consistently ranks highly in global liveability indexes despite being small. What geographic factors most likely contribute to this?
Correct. Wellington's hilly, compact geography — often cited as a constraint — actually supports liveability. Short distances mean people walk more. The harbour and surrounding hills provide green and recreational space. A concentrated city centre means culture, cafes, and work are close together. Constraints can create character.
Wellington's compactness is actually its liveability asset. Because the city is hemmed in by hills and water, it hasn't sprawled — so most things are close together, walkable, and accessible. The harbour and hills create natural recreational space. What seems like a geographic limitation creates a more human-scale city.

Go Deeper

For the Curious

The Auckland vs Wellington Debate. Auckland has 1.7 million people; Wellington has 440,000. Auckland sprawls across a large isthmus, car-dependent and traffic-clogged. Wellington is compact, hilly, and walkable. Yet Auckland generates more economic output. Which city model is better? This isn't a simple question. Think about: environmental impact, housing affordability, quality of life, economic productivity, cultural vitality. Research: "Auckland Wellington urban planning comparison."

Wellington's Te Ara Paparārangi — the proposed cycleway and urban regeneration project along the city's waterfront and hills — is a live debate right now. Some residents want more cycling infrastructure. Others worry about disruption and cost. Meanwhile, Wellington City Council is also dealing with a housing crisis, aging earthquake-prone buildings, and the question of whether to allow taller buildings in the CBD. Look up: "Wellington spatial plan 2024" — this is real urban geography decision-making happening in your own city.

Design Challenge — Your Ideal Neighbourhood

Geographers and city planners think about neighbourhoods in terms of what they provide and for whom. Using what you've learned:

  1. Draw or describe your ideal neighbourhood in Wellington. What does it have? What is missing from your current area?
  2. Consider: housing density, green space, public transport, shops, schools, cycle paths, community spaces.
  3. Research one city in the world that has solved one of these challenges well. What can Wellington learn from it?

A question to sit with: Cities are built by humans — but they shape humans in return. Children who grow up in walkable, green neighbourhoods have measurably different outcomes (health, social connections, independence) than those in car-dependent suburbs. If cities shape who we become, who should get to decide how they're designed — governments, developers, or the people who live in them? And what happens when those groups disagree?
