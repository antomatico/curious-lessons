Lesson 1

Every Animal Has a Family

A house cat and a lion are cousins. So are a dog and a wolf. Let's find out how every animal on Earth is part of one giant family.

Scroll to start↓
The Big Idea

Animals come in kinds — and the kinds are related

Every animal belongs to a kind, called a species. And animals that look alike are usually cousins — they share an old, old grandparent. Join the dots far enough back, and all living things are one enormous family. 🌳

Think about your own family. You might look a bit like your cousin because you share the same grandparents. Animals work the same way! A lion and your pet cat look alike because, long ago, they shared the same animal grandparent.

Scientists love to sort animals into groups — like sorting LEGO by colour. The smallest group is a species: one kind of animal whose babies grow up to be the same kind. A sheep makes baby sheep. A penguin makes baby penguins. Easy!

Spot the family

Surprising animal cousins

Some animals are related in ways that might surprise you:

🐱 ↔ 🦁

Cat & Lion
Both are cats! One just got a lot bigger and louder.

🐕 ↔ 🐺

Dog & Wolf
Your fluffy dog's great-great-grandparent was a wild wolf.

🐑 ↔ 🐐

Sheep & Goat
Close cousins — like brother and sister kinds.

🐔 ↔ 🦖

Chicken & Dinosaur
Birds are the great-grandchildren of dinosaurs. Really!

🥝
Aotearoa connection: New Zealand has the strangest animal family of all. For millions of years almost no furry land animals lived here — just birds, a lizard-like reptile called the tuatara, and two tiny bats (we call them pekapeka). That's why we have amazing birds like the kiwi and kākāpō. We'll find out why in Lesson 4!
📜
A bit of history: People have been sorting animals into family groups for thousands of years. About 250 years ago a scientist named Carl Linnaeus invented the tidy naming system we still use today. The word "species" simply means "a kind."

A challenge for curious minds 🧠
