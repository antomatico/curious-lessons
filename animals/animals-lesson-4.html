Aotearoa's Animals

Native, wild, tame and farmed. Let's sort out New Zealand's animals — and follow the sheep all the way home to our green hills.

The Big Idea

We can sort animals by how they got here and how they live with us

New Zealand's animals fall into four groups: native ones that evolved here, wild ones that people brought and now run loose, tame ones that live with people, and farmed ones raised for food and wool — like our sheep! 🐑
Four groups

Let's sort them out 🗂️

🥝Native

Native

Evolved right here over millions of years: kiwi, kākāpō, takahē, tuatara, and our little bats (pekapeka).

🐀Wild / Introduced

Wild

Brought by people, now living wild and causing trouble: possums, rats, stoats.

🐕Domesticated

Tame

Tame animals that live with people as pets or helpers: dogs, cats, chickens.

🐑Farmed

Farmed

Raised on farms for food, milk and wool: sheep, cows, deer.

A great mystery solved

Why are NZ's native animals mostly birds? 🐦

Remember in Lesson 1, we said New Zealand had almost no native furry land animals? Here's why. About 80 million years ago, New Zealand broke away from the other land and floated off on its own — before most furry mammals even existed!

With no cats, dogs, or other predators to run from, our birds were safe on the ground. Some, like the kiwi and kākāpō, even stopped flying and walked everywhere instead. They had no need for wings! It was a bird paradise. 🌿

⚠️
Why this matters today: When people arrived, they brought animals like rats and stoats. Our birds had never learned to be afraid of them — so many became endangered. That's why New Zealanders work so hard to protect native animals at safe places like Zealandia in Wellington.
The sheep comes home

How the sheep reached New Zealand 🚢

Remember the woolly sheep from Lesson 3 — the one people made from the wild mouflon? Eventually it travelled by ship right across the world to New Zealand. But its start here was very bumpy:

🐑
A funny true story: The famous explorer Captain Cook let the very first sheep loose in New Zealand on 20 May 1773 — but the two sheep ate the wrong plants and died within just a few days! Not a great start. Real sheep farming didn't take off until the 1850s.

After that, though, sheep loved New Zealand's green grassy hills. There got to be SO many that in 1982 there were about 70 million sheep — that's roughly 20 sheep for every single person in the country! 🤯

Today there are fewer — about 23 million — but that's still loads of sheep. So next time you see one on a hillside, you'll know the whole story: it's the great-great-(many greats!)-grandchild of a wild mouflon, changed by people over 11,000 years, and carried all the way to Aotearoa. That is where a sheep comes from!
